  • The key principle from DIT, which I use all the time with AF, is Little and Often.

    This is what makes the Mark Forster systems superior to GTD, from my perspective.

    The key concept for GTD is the Next Action. Before I stop working on something, in GTD, I need to figure out what the next physical action will be. I always had trouble with this back in the day when I was a GTDer, before I ever heard of Mark Forster. The concept of "next physical action" cannot be uniquely defined. "Murder boss?" "Shoot gun?" "Pull trigger?" "Flex right index finger?" There are so many ways to describe an action.

    The great thing about Little and Often, is that I don't have to worry about defining the next action because I know that when it comes time for me to start working on that item, I will redefine the action at the time I begin working on it. I can write anything I want in my list. Let's say I write "Murder boss." From reading the DIT book, I know that I don't actually have to murder him right now, in order to mark the item off, and then re-enter it at the bottom of the list. I merely have to take any action related to the project. I might pull the Murder Boss folder out of my file cabinet and review my notes. I might look in the safe to make sure my revolver is in good working order.

    If I didn't have Little and Often, I don't know if I could get AF to work for me at all. Little and Often is what allows me to make some progress on the kinds of things that, with GTD, I would put off for months, or even years.

  • AF4 vs DIT is an interesting question. Viewing it from my limited knowledge, I see similarities between AF4 and DIT. Both system use a closed and an open list, where the closed list gets most of the focus. In both systems there is a possibility to work on items on the open list. DIT is strongly time bound, as closed lists only exist for a day. Imposing to AF4 the same time constraint(clock) could make the two methods look very similar.
    But that's maybe only for me, as (over)focusing on similarities is often my way of working.
