Resolving some difficulties with AF: Daily Structure & Timeboxing.

  • Hi everyone, I am trying out timeboxing (or timed bursts, as Mark calls them) to try and solve a couple of specific problems I have with AF:

    Problem: Worrying about urgent tasks that have come into my inboxes over night and not being able to deal with them as efficiently as in DIT.
    Solution: Spend upto 90 minutes timed (3 x 30 mins) first thing each morning in clearing these tasks.

    Problem: Starting projects or tasks, but not knowing where to start. (This is not an issue with discrete tasks).
    Solution: Spend upto 30 minutes and then stop.

    Problem: Being disorganised for tomorrow because I've been working from my AF list.
    Solution: Spend upto 60 minutes timed (2 x 30 mins) each evening tidying up / making lunch / preparing for next day meetings etc.

    So, my day has 3 main parts to it - Clearing my inboxes, Autofocus and scheduled meetings and events, Tidy up / make lunch / prepare for next day.

    I know it's not exactly rocket science but I thought it may be of use to others. Has anyone else had success with this type of approach?

  • Hi Leon

    I have definitely found at particular times that there are requirements to address an issue such as described above. However what I have also found is that, as a result of AF seeming to "cause" the issue, it actually highlighted the root of the problem rather than just "create" a problem. For me previous systems did manage things such as email but not always effectively and certainly not easily. AF, by highlighting my resistance, made me think more creatively about the issue rather than finding yet another "just do it" approach.

    Inboxes - I do periodically find my inbox builds up but as I have "check email" on every page I can always action it on a fairly frequent basis. However I periodically (off list) check it just to ensure there is nothing vital. If there is that will be sufficient to ensure the next "check email" task stands out!

    Projects and Tasks - I find that my Project Management system (described elsewhere) enables me to have a reasonably good overview of each project. Although I have always run with a PM system AF has enabled me to redefine it and make it more efficient.

    With regard to the whole "feeling disorganised" scenario, I find that having a list in view (I use a MindMap) of targets for the week, helps me to ensure that I block out time if necessary. Time Blocks are often a necessity where we work to short deadlines but again I feel these can be handled within AF rather than too rigidly. A task "planning for tomorrow" on every page, or something similar can deal with such things.

    With meetings, they are timed events, and therefore the planning element has a deadline. I have found that in some cases, where I have enough notice the planning and preparation can be done easily within AF. In other cases that is not possible and so a Time Block period is required. I personally would recommend not creating a regular Time Block for that purpose but using it as an emergency option only. Otherwise the danger is that you could miss AF's prompting to look at a more systematic solution (eg ensuring that all meetings are scheduled at least a week in advance).

    Leon, I think from your previous posts that you are a lot better at following a disciplined approach than I am so you may not find all of my suggestions to be helpful for your particular requirements, but hopefully something may be :-)

  • Hi Christine,

    You asked: "When you say "most of them are just in the AF list with a (20) or (2H) after them so I know what I intend to do" does that not interfere with the "for as long as you feel" rule or do you just do this for Time Blocking tasks?"

    Yes, and Yes! I find that it is best for me to work on some things in spurts. Take language learning, for example. I can tune the TV or radio to a Spanish speaking station and just be exposed to it for as long as I like. However, to actually accomplish something it takes a block of time. Psychology has gotten a lot of things wrong over the years, but due to the mostly wasted side road taken by the Behaviorists, we did learn a whole ton of useful things about how the mind learns. We know that about 2 hours followed by a break, followed by spaced review has been shown to be the most effective way of learning and retaining what has been learned. Any longer in the study phase is counter productive. Without a break the subconscious does not have time to the the necessary filing and sorting. Without the spaced review, the biochemical links are not renewed and reinforced to make the memory long term. So when I study something, I use what we know works.

    When it comes to something like house cleaning I find that 20 minutes works well. It also works well for practice kinds of things like music performance. I can sit and exercise music for 20 minutes and benefit greatly from it. (If I were having to learn something big, I'd go back to the 2 hour session, though.)

    As to home organization maintenance ... I find that just doing it "little and often" works the best. I pick up things that are out of place and take them to their home as I cruse from room to room on some other errand.

    Of course, I could define "little and often" as 20 minutes ... and maybe it is, philosophically. IOW, many long term projects don't get done because we don't have huge blocks of time available. But we all have 20 minutes we can devote to something every day to make big changes. Exercise is like this. 20 Minutes a day is more efficacious than an hour or more that is never done because we don't have the time.
