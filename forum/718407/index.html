When is a system a system?

  • When is a system a complete system, meaning being self-contained? I see many posts where people say that they love AF, but go on to say things, like I use AF along with my project list, someday maybe list, daily tasks on a post-it etc. Is this really doing AF? In my mind a system whatever it may be should be able to stand on its own as designed and handle all you need to get accomplished, projects, tasks, actions, items you are awaiting a response on etc, without the need for tweaking.

    This is not a slam on AF, because, I believe if you do it as written, it can be all you need with the obvious exception of a calendar. I just wanted to see why people are tweaking AF and not using it as designed and to see what people think about the idea of a pure system. I have always been a believer in simple, under the theory that less is better and the more places something can be, the more places it isn’t meaning you have to review too many things to figure out what you need to do the less effective you are.

  • Hi Gerry,

    I'm going to approach this from a philosophical perspective. When is a system a system? I would first answer the question: What is the scope of the system you are considering?

    No time management system will actually cook dinner for you, will it? Does that make it an incomplete system? Not at all as cooking meals, while something you want to do, is not within the scope of a time management system.

    AF leaves out many things that, IMO, must be done: plan goals, control time related items, handle scheduled and repeating items. Does that make it "incomplete"? I guess it all depends on where you draw the line. I feel that AF does what it says it will do. It is a TOOL which, when used as described, will help manage discretionary time. It is not deficient because it has no rules about planning one's goals. It is silent on that mater because that is not within the scope of the AF system. It is pretty well assumed that you have goals and have planned them.

    My view on this is that all of these "systems" are not really "systems" but tools. Much as a hammer is only one tool, not the entire kit a carpenter needs to do his work. If one comes up with a design for a better hammer he should not be criticized because it won't saw wood!

    My advice would be to use AF in the way designed and fit it into the context of the way you run your life. Use a calendar. Have shopping lists. Have project lists. File reference material. Keep records of how you have done tasks in the past so you don't have to reinvent them. Use all of the time management tools which make sense to you. But if you are using AF, don't tinker with the AF rules so far as working the pages or dismissing items. That will kill the AF system. Other than that, AF lives happily with all of your other tools.

  • Mike:

    <<Build a bomb based on nuclear fission>>

    An interesting example, because of course the scientists who originally were working on nuclear fission had no intention of their work being used to produce a bomb. No one in say 1930 was working to a top-down goal of "build a nuclear bomb that can end the next war by dropping it on two enemy towns." It wasn't until about 1933 that scientists started to realise what the implications of the work for warfare might be - and pretty horrified by them they were.

    The Manhattan project could produce the bomb but the actual ability to use it was completely out of their control because it depended on two other major factors:

    1) An aircraft capable of carrying it.

    2) Having airfields sufficiently close for the aircraft to carry it to the enemy target towns.

    In fact neither of these conditions were available until shortly before the time when the bombs were dropped (August 1945). The heavy-lift B-29s weren't introduced until early 1945, and the Marianas weren't captured until November 1944. Both of these were done with the intention of facilitating conventional bombing, not the nuclear bomb.

    So the use of the atomic bomb was actually literally a case of "winging it" - seizing opportunities as they arose.

    Some years ago I did an exercise with my staff when we decided to identify projects and define job processes. I got everyone to write a list of everything they did or had on a to do list, and to include on that list everything they could think of. I then got them to highlight everything they did as a routine and extract those to a separate list. Those tasks became the basis of a job specification. All other tasks were examined and grouped by type, forming the basis of natural projects. That method of project identification has always seemed incredibly natural to me and whilst we will all have a feel for what projects we are working on, that type of exercise can produce some surprising results.

    Do you know, I think I managed it all without any list of task, steps and deadlines or any time management system like AF. I just did what needed doing at the time. It would certainly have been easier if I'd had AF then, but I can't imagine that a list of the type you mention would have been any use at all.

    What I'd probably do these days if I had to go through a similar experience is put the individual actions into AF as they came up, but also have a generic "House Move" task, which would be a chance to do a batch of actions together. That way I could do the actions separately or together as I felt like at the time.
