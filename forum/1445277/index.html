Fifteen Q&As for reducing anxiety with SuperFocus

  • Herewith I present a bunch of questions and answers to myself, about how to reduce anxiety while using SF. Many of these ideas are gleaned from Mark and other contributors to this forum. For brevity and due to my own laziness, I haven't included specific attributions or links to earlier posts. Hope this helps others; just writing it all down helped me. ;-)

    Comments, criticisms, additional Q&As are all welcome!

    -----------------------------
    1. How can I remember where I am, and whether I've done any C1 tasks? Mark and seek a 'lonely' dot: place a dot to the left of the next chosen task, as usual. When completed, don't join the cross-out line or connect the dots vertically, until you're ready to move onto the next page.

    2. If I'm taking a break, how do I mark the next task? Use a pencil to make the dot. When you come back, you can erase the dot if a different task calls out to you.

    3. How can I jump to a task on another page, without breaking the rules? Add a duplicate entry to C2 on the current page, as an "urgent" task. Cross out the original.

    4. How can I motivate myself to clear the page completely? Push to do just a little something on each remaining task. Visualize the cleared page.

    5. How can I consolidate many pages with only a few tasks remaining? As in the previous suggestion, push yourself to do a little on each & feel good about clearing the page. Or just laugh off any one of these pages – RDRR: Relax, Dismiss, Review, Reenter (RDRR = haR Dee haR haR!).

    6. How do I separate two contexts (e.g. Home and Work)? Use the stalactite-stalagmite method (a.k.a. cave deposits). Write work-related tasks from the top down in each column. Write home-related tasks from the bottom up. Draw a heavy or colored horizontal line to separate when the column is full.

    7. How can I avoid dismissing a page with many tasks remaining? Choose the least painful task and do a little something on it. Or add a question mark (?) to a task's description, making deciding not to do it a valid way to complete it. Also note that skipping a page with only out-of-context tasks in C1 is allowed; in this case, you don't need to dismiss (yet).

    8. How can I avoid getting stuck on one page? Use C2 wisely (only for truly urgent tasks and those you are truly committed to finishing). Also, you can set a timer for each remaining task, or for turning the page.

    9. What is the best way to deal with a big task? The answer depends on what will work for you, and which of two methods will cause less anxiety. If it's important and you want to focus on finishing it, you can use C2 reentry, always listing the task in the same way. If it's less important to finish soon, but you still don't want to end up dismissing it, break it into many small Next Actions (NAs), and put each next action into C1, instead of listing it as one big task. The choice of how to do this important, but both strategies can work.

    10. What other tools should I have to make my SF notebook fully functional? Use a multicolor pen/pencil, a 43-folders "tickler" file, A-Z reference files, In and Pending trays for organizing current paperwork, and a calendar for appointments only.

    11. How can I postpone task entry? Put the task description by itself on a card or sheet of paper and place it in your tickler file, in the folder for the day (or month) when you want it to be entered into your SF system. For big multitask projects, make separate project lists of Next Actions, and enter only a single NA for each project into SF.

    12. How can I "link" my paper-based list to emails, files, etc.? Keep a date-based email archive (monthly, quarterly, whatever), and write the email date and/or file location in [square brackets] as part of the task description. You can also just print out the email or other computer document and put it in your Pending tray and/or tickler file.

    13. How can I make using SF more fun? Add easy tasks. Also enter fun/break/exercise activities as tasks. Don't forget to browse and/or post to this website.

    14. How can I stay in the flow? Use a pocket notebook so you can always carry your list for reference and emptying your head. Pencil-in the next dot when on break and you're not sure which will be your next task when you get back. Remember the little-and-often principle. Don't be afraid to dismiss a page – you can always reenter tasks after reviewing them. Minimize the use of C2.

    15. How can I track my progress? At the beginning of each day, write the date to the right of the first new entry. Leave 'lonely' dots on the current page until you are ready to turn to the next one. Occasionally make side counts of number of active pages, number of remaining tasks, etc.
    -----------------------------

  • Here are some additional tips / rule variants for moving more quickly through the list (listed in order of effectiveness, at least in my experience):

    - When starting a new page, finish processing all C2 items before processing any C1 items (or before even looking at any C1 items!)

    - Set a time limit for each page. E.g., if you have 12 pages and want to cycle through them all in 8 hours, set a timer for 45 minutes every time you start a new page.

    - Enter urgent items in C2 on the NEXT page, not C2 on the current page

    - Use a notebook with more lines per page
