  • Hi, Jupiter,

    I'm a longtime mostly lurker on this forum. I have read your posts, including this one, with interest. Happy New Year to you and to everybody on this wonderful forum!

    I, too, have gone back from paper to OmniFocus. The reason is that paper was becoming too cumbersome and unfocused. Of course, any digital solution can also do the same.

    Just like you, I have found the double reading to be important onscreen. Other things that help me stay on track are flagging a small subset of things to work on (à la 3T) and making sure that I keep my computer desktop free of clutter and don't collect too many open browser windows with "open loops" of stuff that I'm interested in but haven't decided what to do with.

    For organizing my tasks in OF, I have a few projects and contexts. I'm not fussy about creating a new project for everything that has multiple steps, as GTD would require. Thank heaven for Mark Forster's various systems, which have helped me to simplify and only complicate where absolutely necessary. I only have contexts for things that I need to see a separate list for, for example, errands. (That list I use in OF on my iPod Touch when I'm out.)

    To display my task list, I group and sort in Context mode by Date added. This gives me a list somewhat like DWM, with separate groups for tasks added today, this week, within the month, and longer. Any tasks in the last group are ripe for dismissal. I work those subgroups sort of like AF4R.

    Thanks for giving me the prod to write up my system. I hope it's coherent.

    Cheers,
    Lenore

  • Just a note on the many browser windows thing.

    I struggled a long time with that because one online research goes to the next and before you know it, you have about 15 windows/tabs about the various "loose ends" of you research. It's kind of a mind map where you want to read all the boundary nodes but you really don't have to. The problem is, you don't know where the center of your research is so you test the boundaries this way.

    I also happen to just be interested in a totally different thing while I'm searching for a specifc thing. The remedy to my problem was to use "Instapaper":
    http://www.instapaper.com/

    You basically register but you don't even have to put a password to register!
    And you drag a button to your browser bookmarks bar.
    When you get to a page you might want to read but is not in your direct field of focus, you just click that bookmark and it saves the link to the page on Instapaper. You can then peruse your findings after the event leaving you focused when you have to and flirting with knowledge when you want to ;)
