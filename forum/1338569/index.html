Why Systems Breakdown

  • I saw in another post Mark mentioned returning to AF1. It got me thinking, recently the forum exploded with a bunch of new systems and of course the usual tweaks and add ons to those systems. Yet, many do not persist for long periods of time. I am sure some of it is the curiosity of the new, but I also think many systems just breakdown due to complexity. the complexity of trying to have a system do everything or handle each possibility. With complex systems comes the overhead of maintaining the system. The reality is each moment spent organizing and managing time is a moment spent not doing something that needs to be done.

    I have found simply writing in one place (a cheap notebook) got me 80% of the productivity of a complex system like GTD with virtually no overhead. Has anyone else found a simple principle that have provided a huge benefit or like to comment on the issue of complexity?

    Gerry

  • Complex systems do tend to break down, but I don't recall reports of anyone struggling with these new systems which really aren't all that complex anyway.

    Simple principle that worked for me: To build a new habit, attach it to an old habit.

    For example: write it into AutoFocus, or post it in your bedroom (I always get up in the morning...).

    They say it takes 30 days to establish a habit, but following the above principle it's trivial to keep many things going for 30 days until it's ingrained.

  • leon:

    << What signal do you have in your notebook to show there are no more outstanding tasks on a page in your notebook? >>

    My practice with all forms of AF and its derivatives has been to write a cross (X) on the top outside corner of a finished page and to put a circle round it when there are no earlier pages with outstanding tasks.

    Putting a vertical line in the left margin which joins the horizontal deletion marks also helps one to see immediately how many tasks are on a page.

  • Leon,
    I use a notebook with squares rather than lines. To the left of each entry I draw in a narrow column and after working on a task I fill in the square beside it - this makes it really easy to see unactioned tasks, and when the page is complete you have a fully filled-in column. It would work equally well on lined, rather than squared paper.
    Best wishes.
