  • mit,

    I came to Mark Forster initially from a post about him on David Allen's website. So, I consider most of my delegated tasks as "waiting fors," i.e., I am waiting for someone else to do something. One thing I love about electronic implementations is that I can have a field which holds information on other people. That way, for example, if John calls me on the phone, while I am talking to him I can filter my list for his name and then I can ask him for updates on the five things he promised to do for me.

    I think DWM works well for this. At least once a week I will contact the person to ask them to update the status on whatever it is they are supposed to be doing.
    27 February 2010 at 20:39 | moises
  • My list tells me what to do, so if I delegate a task, that's an action, it moves to next week, and is rewritten to say who's working on it.

    If the thing comes back to me done, I can easily find it among the 30-60 things on this week and next week.

    If I see the task again and want to know progress, I will check up on progress. This system forces me to not let anything slide more than a week so it's perfect.

  • I'm using Outlook Tasks to manage my DWM list. For delegated tasks, I add a "category". I have 2 main category groups. One set for projects so that I can aggregate tasks across a project (useful when checking plans and preparing progress updates). Another set is delegated/my team. Like moises, when I'm with someone I can easily look at tasks related to that person as a reminder. This is not just delegated tasks, but also "talk about X" and "arrange meeting with Y" type tasks that I have not yet actioned.

    I use various views in my Outlook task list - the main DWM view is set to show only tasks that havent expired, and are within a month of expiry. I use Outlook's "due date" field as the expiry date - I can happily ignore the fact that it doesnt have anything to do with the real task "due date".
