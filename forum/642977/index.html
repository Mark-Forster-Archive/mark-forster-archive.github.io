  • Gupta:

    Each page is a closed list, except for the last one, which is open.

    When you are using the list as one long list in the way you are doing, you are comparing each item with all the other items on the list. That means that there is a natural tendency to go for the easier or simpler items.

    But with AF you only comparing each item with the other items on the same page. Your range of choice is much narrower. So for instance my first active page contains only two items "Sort CDs" and "Prepare Friday's seminar". This means I have the following choices: do both items; do one of the items and leave the other for the next visit to the page; or do neither of the items and dismiss both of them.

  • Kenny

    Agree with Mike re the highlighting - if you only highlight at the point specified in the instructions then only non-active pages will have highlighted items on. If you highlight an item as "needing to be done next" you are effectively rationally grading the urgency of that item and not working with the item "that stands out". Additional highlighting or notation can affect the effectiveness of AF in that it disrupts the scanning of items.

    Give it a try exactly as written for a while and then see if you feel differently.

    Actually, with regard to someday items I think there can be value in adding them into your list. Not necessarily all of them, and not in one go, but if you add them in you may find that, rather than dismiss them you may take action on them and start to progress them. And if not, it's no big deal. They just stay on your lists as "dismissed" and will be reviewed under your periodic review of dismissed items, when they can be re-added if you wish.
