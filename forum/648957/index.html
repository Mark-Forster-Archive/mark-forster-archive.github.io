Autofocus work v home

  • Just wanted to update you on how I'm finding the autofocus method.

    First the good news: it works like a dream in the office. My work is not overly date or deadline based, but is an ongoing set of items that frequently change. Adding everything to one master list has really helped me to reduce stress at work, as I know that nothing I need to do will be forgotten. Not only that, but I love crossing off a task and adding it to the bottom of the list when I've done some work on it, even though I haven't finished it. It keeps the list dynamic and me motivated. Best of all, when I'm bored of looking at something, I just scan the list for something smaller and more manageable to do to pass the rest of the afternoon, or to get me through a patch of boredom :-) It's without a doubt the best method I've ever used in the workplace to control my own work, and has really helped me to feel like I have everything under control.

    Now the bad news: at home it is a completely different story. I still haven't actually completed a single page of items. I am reluctant to put aside those things which I don't want to do, because they do still need doing, hence the list just depresses me when I look at it, as it seems like an endless set of insurmountable tasks around the home and garden. I've done some of the more enjoyable things - reading books I've listed etc., but there are huge sections of list which just don't ever budge. I'm not sure what it is about the home list that makes it so different from the work list, but as a start I think there are two relevant issues:

    1) At work the list is on my desk, and in my line of sight all day. At home I can come home, make dinner, do some stuff around the house, slump exhausted in front of the TV and go to bed, without ever actually opening the notebook.

    2) At work the tasks are all very similar - they involve either talking to someone, or doing something to progress a project. They are easy to define and break down into small to-dos. At home the tasks are so diverse - read a book, finish tiling the bathroom, plastering in the lounge, hang a roller blind, organize digital photos, go for a run. Each item seems like a much bigger undertaking than anything on my work list, and a much bigger effort to actually start.

    I think what I have realized is that the majority of things I feel I should be doing outside of work, I don't actually want to do at all. Ever. Maybe I need some kind of major lifestyle overhaul :-)

    Obviously I'll be sticking to this at work, probably forever!
    I'll try it for a little longer at home - if I have any breakthroughs I will update.

  • Faye:

    To get the right effect from AF it's important to look at each page as a unit, as you say. But it's also important to go through the page looking at each item in turn individually. Don't just skim the page until you find something nice to do.

    I think from what you say that you may still not be doing the system quite right. You only HAVE to do one thing from a page each time you visit it (though normally you would do more). Items only get dismissed from a page if you don't do ANY of them on a visit to that page.

  • Hi Faye
