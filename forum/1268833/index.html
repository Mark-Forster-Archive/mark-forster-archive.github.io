Team Focus?

  • Untested idea. Might not work. Probably needs tweaking.

    Picture a whiteboard with post-it notes, which represents something like an AF4 list. A typical board fits 10 down, 14 across. Imagine there's two and a half columns of post-its, then a marker-drawn horizontal line (closed list) , then 4 more columns of post-its (open list). The team
    - looks at the board,
    - examines items one by one, and
    - each person may select something to work on by grabbing the post-it.
    - When everybody has something, flag the last scanned spot.

    When anybody finishes or gets stuck, they
    - return to the board,
    - If there's more to do, repost the card at the list-end, note the new status
    - if there's no more to do, post the card in a Done area
    - post new cards if new ideas come up, and then
    - pick a new item starting from the flag.

    If, per rules AF rules, tasks need to be dismissed,
    - move these to a dismissal area where the team or manager can review them.
    - Draw a new line for a new closed list.
    - Wrap the list around the board when you hit the right wall.

  • Seraphim I agree screen resolution is limited. We use a whiteboard.

    We are attempting to follow a Kanban at work. To clarify some confusion:
    1. It isn't tasks that go on the team board, but rather standardized items of a particular variety. In our case the items are expected software behaviours. "saving files"
    The tasks are defined by a process represented by columns, applied to each work item. For example, "investigate. Define. Code. Test. Document. Ship"
    so obviously team kanban doesn't cover everything, just the core functions of the team.
    2. Kanban insists on limiting work in progress. A suggested ideal is that # of cards = 2x # of people. So hundreds won't come up.

    Aside from this, there is a backlog of inactive items, which Kanban offers no advice on choosing, and everyone has his own way of tracking everything that doesn't fit the primary function.

    I still seek a system that helps with those 2 issues.

    This is all very interesting because I'm trying a new twist on DWM right now where I have two "hot lists" that contain only my top, primary work and my top, primary personal tasks. These are the tasks that I "know" will make the most difference, the ones that keep pushing on me, the ones I keep resisting, the ones my manager (and at home, my wife) are always asking me about. I give each of them its own DWM list, and spend most of the day, outside of meetings, focusing on the Work Hotlist, and most of the evening and weekend, outside of family time, focusing on the Personal Hotlist. I have then been blocking out meetings with myself for handling maintenance and miscellaneous, each with its own DWM list.

    At first I started panicking because I couldn't see what was on the lists I wasn't currently looking at. But I stopped panicking when I remembered that I had already blocked out time on my calendar to deal with those things. Each list has several time blocks per week on my calendar, so they won't get dropped.

    This is the same way I handled all my regular meetings with other people, such as my biweekly 1:1 with my manager -- I just kept a DWM list of all the stuff I needed to discuss with him, and went through each item on the list when we met -- or at least whatever "stood out". If something never stood out, then it dropped off the list, and we never discussed it -- which was fine.

    This way of handling ongoing meetings worked so well, I decided to set up meetings with myself to do the same thing. I've only been doing it a few days and I like it so far -- but then again, I like every new approach for the first few days. :-) Time will tell if this actually works for me or not.
