Can SF3 handle a "two teams" situation?

  • I'm using SF3 since 2 weeks after 2 years of mostly AF1. I'm now confronted with a situation that raises some questions in my mind.

    I'm currently transitioning from one team to a new one. We have an arrangement where, during a 4 months transition period, I work in my old team on some days of the week and in my new team on the remaining days. (E.g. for March this means Mon, Tue & Thu in the old team and Wed & Fri in new team.) Sometimes there can be exceptions, when I spend some time on an urgent request from one team on a day of the other team. And then I have some activities that are not specific to any of the 2 teams.

    This creates a problem in SF3, especially in Column 2 and with dismissal.

    For Column 2, the rules require that we work on all items before we can turn to the next page. If C2 contains unfinished tasks from Team 1, and I'm on a Team 2 day, then I'm not supposed to work on them that day, and I can't move ahead.

    Similarly, if Column 1 only contains Team 1 activities and I'm on a Team 2 day, this means I should dismiss the page, whereas on a different day I'd just start working on the tasks.

    I thought about drawing a line in the middle of C1, having the both halves of the page reserved for one of the teams, and ignoring the part of the colums that is not relevant that day. But this still leaves the non-team specific tasks: where do I put them? Similarly, having 2 parts in C2 could work for unfinished tasks, but an urgency from the "wrong" team should still be handled by C2 anytime. Using alternating Team 1 and Team 2 pages is essentially similar.

    What would be a good approach to handle this (temporary) situation?

  • Marc (from Brussels) wrote:
    <<< If C2 contains unfinished tasks from Team 1, and I'm on a Team 2 day, then I'm not supposed to work on them that day, and I can't move ahead. >>>

    I have the same problem since I keep work and personal items in the same list. Sometimes I am at work and I can't do the personal items in Column 2.

    Mark Forster recommends moving forward Column 2 items that can't physically be done -- so that's the rule I follow.

    However, our situations are slightly different. I usually have time for both work and personal tasks every day. In your situation, you may go for a series of days without working on one of your teams. So, you'd be forced to carry along those unactionable C2 tasks through many pages. That seems impractical -- lots of rewriting -- and it would also cause C2 to fill up quickly.

    Maybe you could do this instead: If you are working for Team 1, then copy your Team 2 items to a special page in the back of your notebook, and enter "Team 2 items" in C2. Whenever you can't work on your Team 2 items, just copy that task forward to C2 on the next page.. When it comes time to switch from Team 1 to 2, copy your Team 2 tasks to your active C2 from the back of the book, and move your Team 1 C2 tasks to the back of the book.

    It sounds like a lot of rewriting but I have found in practice that these kinds of things take a LOT less time to implement than it takes to describe them.

  • Marc, you might try something like this.

    Have two lists, one for the days you're sitting with T1, and one for the days you're sitting with T2.

    Don't worry about making the lists mutually exclusive--i.e., it's OK if the same task appears in both lists.

    At the beginning of a day when you're sitting with T1, do a scan of your complete T2 list and copy any tasks that you think you need to/want to do that day onto the T1 list. That would include non-team specific tasks. Then you can work the T1 list without worrying that you're missing something from the other list.

    At the beginning of a day when you're sitting with T2, do the opposite. Then you can focus on the T2 list until you switch contexts again. If you encounter items while working the T2 list that were done on one of your T1 days, just cross them off.
