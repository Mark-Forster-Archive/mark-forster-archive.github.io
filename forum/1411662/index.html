Home/Office Mixed Lists - Dismissal and Column 2 Rules

  • I have one list for home and office tasks (since I can do a lot of tasks at both places). I see two possible treatments here when it comes to dismissal/column 2 block (I mean the rule that all tasks in column 2 have to be actioned) due to the problem of "wrong place" (not be able to action the task because I am at the wrong place):

    1) ignorance: I simply pretend that the "wrong place" task does not exist. Consequence: I have unactioned column 2 tasks on more than two pages.
    2) push forward: At least for column 2 tasks - if I remember correctly - Mark recommends to cross the "wrong place" task out and put it on next page's column 2

    What do you think? Mark, may I ask why you recommend method 2 (you might have tried both)?

  • I was trying an experiment with "work" and "personal" pages interspersed in one SF book. During "work" time, if I came to a "personal" page, I might cherry-pick an easy item or two -- especially urgent personal phone calls and the like, that needed to be during work hours anyway -- but otherwise I would not apply any SF rules. And likewise for work pages during personal time.

    It worked OK, but there was always a loss of momentum -- the Column 2 effect seemed to get diluted whenever I switched from work to personal or vice-versa.

    Late last week, I decided to try something new: write all tasks in one notebook with no regard for whether they are work or personal, and follow all the rules at all times. This has worked pretty well -- especially when following Mark's rule of copying forward any Column 2 items that physically cannot be done. I count some things in this category such as any lengthy personal projects that would seriously interfere with my work if I undertook them during business hours -- it's actually physically possible but completely contrary to my obligation to my employer.

    In Column 1, I am writing work items from the top of the page, going down; and personal items, from the bottom of the page, going up, and then drawing a heavy line where they meet in the middle. This one mechanic is similar to the "stalagmite/stalactite" method that many of us used for the AF1-4. However, the approach I am taking right now is that I am writing it this way just to get a visual cue of whether something is "work" or "personal" and to sort of group things together. And that's the ONLY purpose. I am NOT using this stalagmite/stalactite method for dismissal. (We used to dismiss work items only during work hours and personal items only during personal time -- we would not dismiss any of the personal items during work time, nor any of the work items during personal time.)

    I decided instead to follow all the SF rules absolutely strictly. This means that if I dismiss the tasks in Column 1 for any reason during work time, then I dismiss ALL the tasks in Column 1, including personal tasks. It's only Column 2 items that get "moved forward" if they can't "physically" or "morally" be done right now.

    Essentially this is just standard SF, exactly what Mark does himself, except I make the visual distinction between work and personal. The reason I am elaborating on it so much is that I've experimented with (and struggled with) many different ways to handle "work" and "personal" tasks in my "corporate job but work from home most of the time" situation. And I've finally come full circle back to the original rules as written.

    There's at least one reason why it seems to be working pretty well over the last few days. I have lots of personal commitments, but they tend to pile up because my work can be very demanding. The problem with keeping the work and personal separated is that my personal tasks tend to grow and grow and grow. They always far outnumber my work tasks. I realized that a big reason for this is that I wouldn't dismiss any of those items unless I was on "personal time" -- and that just didn't happen often enough to make much progress on those items. During the start-up phase (regardless of the system), I'd keep up pretty well, but eventually the backlog would just grow and grow -- I'd keep adding new personal tasks, but never have the time to let the system process them.

    Combining everything into one list, and allowing the system to dismiss things aggressively if needed, works really well because it gives me a more realistic idea of how much actual personal time I have to commit to all these personal tasks. It makes me realize that if I want to keep up with all my personal commitments, I need to work more "reasonable" hours, improve my health, or find some other way to have more time for them. Or, finally concede that I can't do all of these things and seriously scale back.

    Anyway, just some thoughts... I'll let you know how it works out over the next few weeks.
