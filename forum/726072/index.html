Report from Studio 13 (2)

  • Having been using AF from the beginning, it is working well now at the Studio. I have 7 active pages and have dismissed 10, all of 35 items. I have started several new projects and revived some moribund ones, in one case after a gap of twenty years. Kudos to Mark for putting together this method.

    Outside the Studio the picture is less clear. My Office list has 11 active pages and 13 inactive. I struggle to visit every page daily. The situation with my Home list is stretched still more, with 26 active pages and only 1 dismissed. As such, recently added tasks are getting neglected, which were formally handled by DIT.

    I am thus thinking of taking a leaf from Mel's and Charles' book and starting each day at the end of the list and then continuing from where I left off the previous day. But I would tweak that idea by going to the penultimate page rather than the final, and "touching" the final two pages before reverting to the previous day's bookmark. This would be to handle the situation where there are very few items on the final page.

    I prefer not to change systems, although the above represents a simple additional rule. Systems can sometimes work like a Procrustean bed, on which users had to be adjusted lengthwise rather than the bed, I'll try to make AF1 work for a bit longer before switching. Either way, after 3 months use, I conclude that AF is an important step forward.

  • What is the normal operation of AF when the amount of time available for doing is less than the amount of time needed, and you keep on adding items?
    Answer - the number of pages will increase and when you reach a certain point you will hit each page, even the new one, relatively infrequently.

    Solutions:-
    1. Keep the pages and items moving; even so far as possibly only doing 1 item a page and that for 5 minutes (when it then goes on the last page). This brings the last page around sooner, but also mixes older items with newer ones as the ones that have been worked on are written on the last page.
    2. When you realise that the amount of time needed exceeds that available, keep your eye open for items that stand out for being too low a priority to get done, and dismiss them.
    I'm sure there are many others.

    The only reason why you should be anxious about not doing recently input items that fast is if the issue is urgency (probably needs to be tackled another way) or if recent items are genuinely more important (in which case many of the older items really need to be dismissed).

    Focussing on the recent will always feel better (we tend to think that what is in our minds now is more important) - but after a gap do they still feel that they were that important? Frequently not.

    Done (mostly) according to the instructions, AF is pretty much a self-regulating system. The more it is tweaked, the less it has the capacity to self-regulate. If operating it is leading to stress, it should best be viewed as a call to reflect and refine our 'intuitive' decision making rather than a need to tweak the system. Of course, our individual circumstances may mean that some tweaks will suit us best but I do agree with Mark about the system 'as is' functioning better than any changes.

  • Hi Laurence

    My "every page" tasks started accidentally in that when I changed from a 34 line notebook to a 38 page lined book I needed 4 items to "fill the gap" to maintain page structure. I wanted to rewrite the pages as the reason for the change was that the pages kept falling out of my original book :-)

    Personally I don't see that as breaking the rules - I have no problem with adding an item twice to maintain flow, in the same way that I would not check back to see if a task I think of has been added previously. If something is duplicated it just gets crossed out when reached. I take your point about the likeness to an index card but it is not really the same in that there is no "requirement" to action it/them every day but still only when/if they stand out. The key to AF is the standing out - I can add anything on to my lists, but even if I add an item 20 times, if it does not stand out then it still gets dismissed (20 times!). With an index card of "must do" items that would not be the case.

    You are absolutely right about the final pages becoming "the list". That is a real danger with that approach. When I have have dumped a whole project batch of tasks into my list and worked on them as a batch of pages - as an emergency measure - it has been clear to see the temptation to work on current items. However it is important to remember that one of the strengths of AF is not just the doing of tasks but the fact that it filters those tasks. One of the ways it does that is by putting space between the task entry and the task action, so by not moving from that last page you are losing that benefit.

    I really find that the benefit of any sort of index card approach, whether it be an actual index card, or a MindMap, is not in the card/map itself, and certainly not in the actioning of tasks from that extracted list, but in the writing and identification of the tasks you put on that lists. All it is is a safety net. Our intuition knows what the important things are, our rational mind is not convinced of that fact. By pacifying our rational mind with a list, we are effectively reassuring it that we have given our intuition a reminder so it does not forget. And guess what - it doesn't!

    The answer to everything is 42, er no sorry I meant, "TRUST THE SYSTEM" :-)
