Why I Have Not Tried SF

  • The real reason I haven't tried SF is the DWM.xls works well enough and I just no longer have the inclination to disrupt my extant systems to try every new thing that rolls down the pike.

    But the real reason isn't very interesting.

    Here's a more interesting reason.

    I have found that "little and often" is the most powerful of Mark's principles. Like all great ideas, it had been said before. How many times have we heard that the most important thing is to just get started? But Mark's presentation of this idea was most frabjous.

    Pre-Mark, I would put things on my list and they would sit there (literally) for years. Mark's systems were always good for getting me to clean out those stale items.

    But, as Mark acknowledges, Mark's systems were not always the greatest for getting things completed. Many times I have opened the folder, read a little bit, closed the folder, and re-entered the item. That's the downside of little and often.

    Along comes SF and it is supposed to be based on little and often, yet encourage completion. I just can't fit both those ideas in my head in the same place at the same time.

    If I know that I MUST complete my column 2 items or look at them again in, possibly, a few minutes, I am going to have a very hard time convincing myself that I'll just start on this little task. I will straight away have higher levels of resistance.

    What I liked about little and often, the way Mark presents it, is that it is such an effective psychological trick. The fact is that sometimes, true, I do open the file folder and then put it away again. But, more times than not, once I start something I have overcome the most power obstacle--inertia--and I have necessarily created momentum sufficient to generate some serious progress. If the item is in column 2 and the rules are that I must complete it, I can't tell myself, "I don't have to complete this, I'll just take a little peek at it."


    So, I haven't tried SF because I do not want to break with my conception of little and often by populating my list with tasks requiring completion.

    I recognize that talking about a system is not nearly as effective as using a system. But the reality is that I won't use a system unless I think that the benefit of making the switch is worth the trouble. Please convince me that I have made a conceptual or practical error.

  • Hi moises,

    First, just to make sure it's clear, I will reiterate what others have already said. You don't need to COMPLETE the items in C2. You just need to take some action on them before you can leave the page. If it's not completed, then you do move it forward to C2 on the next page.

    So, you still do "little and often", but C2 keeps you doing that repeatedly on the same tasks until they are done.


    << If I know that I MUST complete my column 2 items or look at them again in, possibly, a few minutes, I am going to have a very hard time convincing myself that I'll just start on this little task. I will straight away have higher levels of resistance. >>

    Looking at a C2 item "again in a few minutes" is normally pretty rare with SF. It depends how fast you turn the page.

    If you have 7-10 pages and you go through them 1-3 times per day (which seems pretty typical) then that works out to about 20 to 90 minutes spent on each visit to a page. So, you'd be forced to deal with this task in C2 every 20 to 90 minutes, on average.

    If you're a slow page-turner like me, you might be forced to deal with that C2 task 2-4 times per day.

    Either way, even for the hardest tasks, I might do the "open the file" 1-minute trick once or twice, but then that's enough to break down the resistance so the next time I see the task I actually make some significant progress. And once you've got some momentum going on a project like that, I've found it can actually help me stay "in the zone". I take little breaks with each page turn, dealing with a few C1 items, but then returning to the main C2 task and just keeping working through it. After a day of that you can really make great progress on some pretty large tasks.

    Smaller tasks (like the regular clearing out of the inbox) get done in 1-3 passes for me. First pass is often "I don't want to deal with this" and so I just open my inbox and close it again. Second pass is "OK let me spend 5 minutes here" and I prune a few things (and prime my subconscious for the third pass). Third pass is "Ok enough already just get it done".


    I was also an avid DWM user, for almost a year, but I can honestly say DWM didn't do "little and often" or drive things to completion nearly as well as SF.

    With DWM, items get pushed out to Today+7 repeatedly till they are done. For large items, every time I pushed it out to Today+7, I almost guaranteed that I'd have built up resistance to it again by the time I looked at that task again. Here is what often happened: The project is going to expire today unless I take action, so I open the file (and close it again). OK, that allows me to push it out to Today+7 again. I'll probably see the task again today, but I can just skip over it, no big deal. I don't REALLY have to deal with it for a whole week! So, several days or a week go by, and now I've really got to deal with it again (or face dismissal). So, I do the "open file" trick again. And several days go by. Finally I am going to miss a hard external deadline so I HAVE to get it done. So I just power through it and get it done. NOT in the zone. NOT very happy about it.

    For small items, it's even worse. If I was resisting them in DWM, they'd just sit on the list for 30 days and then I'd be forced to deal with it or let it be dismissed. If I HAD to get it done, I'd do it -- but again, NOT in the zone, NOT very happy about it. If I tried the "open the file" trick, I could postpone little tasks indefinitely. I could avoid clearing out my email for weeks (cherry picking for so-called "urgent" or "important" emails).


    So my conclusion is that SF actually does little-and-often far more effectively than DWM. I hope this helps convince you to give it a try.
