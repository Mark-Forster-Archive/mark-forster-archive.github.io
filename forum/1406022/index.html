Reconciling Project vs Programme and Organizational vs Continuous

  • Sure there are definitions in place for the difference between a project and a program (For starters, it might be of use commit to a difference in meaning between the English and the American spelling). But I find these distinctions to be interpretive and subjective for the most part. In one view, a program is a collection of projects, in another sense a program is the steering towards intended outcomes. There seems to be a distinction of scope, but like the notions of Long Term and Short Term in economics, this distinction is easily misused. These definitions to me feel a bit loose, as if the model isn't really picturing what's really happening in reality as clearly as it could. And would you separate operational activities from these two categories?

    Do you think Forster's classification of Continuous vs Organizational projects could be of use? Would this make for more concise definition? For example, a program could be redefined as a project focused on the establishment of continuous activities, and a project could be defined as the organization of tasks towards a finite goal.

    So take this into the field, the declaration of Homeland Security as a new branch of government (Sorry to immediately put us in the throws of a political debate, I just think it's a great case study) would be considered the announcement of a program. Under my definition, the aim is to establish a dynamic set of systems whereby continuous security routines (and operational activities) are put into motion, and steered accordingly. Establishing a trapezoid in the pentagon as central headquarters would be a project. Organizationally, there are many functioning systems that need to be put in place, nonetheless there is a clear deliverable and a notion of it being on schedule.

    Would you like to play with these terms a bit with me?
