  • For linking tasks to goals I often use an advice from David Allen.
    Write: _task/action_ re: _goal/project_.

    E.g. "call Bill re: this month's budget", "clear deck re: weekend party", "draft report re: safety inspection", "call auto shop re: tires", etc.

  • I was using Toodledo with AF for a long time. I had as a task on my AF list "Add goals to list." This meant I was to take time to go through each task and assign it to a goal via Toodledo. I quit doing it. I think one reason I quit was because some of the goals didn't have a due date. For me, amorphous goals with no end in sight aren't motivating. Of course, many would argue they aren't goals at all and I wouldn't put up a fuss. ;-) Obviously a second reason is the goals stopped being important to me.

    For projects with due dates that require a good amount of time, I am using DoThisFirst on the iPhone. I think this has replaced my goals accounting I was doing on Toodledo. I love that the program tells me how much time I should commit today to achieve my goal/project by when it's due. The program also tells me how much progress I've made toward the goal/project, but without requiring me to detail every tiny step. There's none of that in fact. I had a goal of tagging all my digital photos by the end of August. Putting this into AF has meant I've done it a few times--not nearly enough to achieve my goal. Now that I am using this approach, I will set a new goal with a new due date and will put it into DoThisFirst to see if I make progress. If I don't, it's obviously not a goal I'm willing to keep pursuing. I allow myself to work either DTF or AF as I choose during the day.

    To use a DTF approach on paper, you would need a separate list of due-dated, time-intensive goals or projects. Estimate the number of hours it will take you to complete the project and a percentage of buffer time you want. For ease of explanation, let's say you have a goal/project to be completed a month from now. If that project will take approximately 30 hours to complete, you'd need to spend an hour a day on it. But if you know you won't be able to work every day (and who will?), you need to factor that in. If you have a 25% buffer, you'd need to work an hour and fifteen minutes on the project every day.

    The rebel procrastinator in me does sometimes look at that figure and say naaaaah. But the logical part of me realizes what I'm in for every day that goes by that I do not work on it. I can no longer use ignorance the way I once did to manage my checking account. ;-) This is an obvious process that we've all used for special circumstances, but could benefit from using more often.
