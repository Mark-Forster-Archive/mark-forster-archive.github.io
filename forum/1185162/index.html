    This is a good observation, of course, and Mark's "little and often" strategy is brilliant at ensuring you do move forward on your projects.     I find that if I follow Mark's advice and break a large project down, listing one or more small tasks in my list alongside all the other daily tasks I have to do, I end up doing one or two things on a project each day, and think that I've made good progress because at least the project is moving forward, without dropping the ball on the countless other tasks I have to do (like emptying my email inbox or staying up-to-date with the volunteer work I do). But if I keep doing this day after day, I end up missing the project's deadline because my progress on the project wasn't anything like fast enough.

    Given that I can't do all my daily tasks plus work enough on the project, I have to balance it somehow. To date, my "strategy" has been to ignore my task list entirely and just work on the project, sometimes day and night to meet a deadline. This means I do hit the deadlines, but don't get anything else done at all. So I end up jumping from fighting fires in one area to fighting fires in another area, ignoring one project after the other to meet the closest deadline, switching frantically between them, and never getting to any of the lower-priority items that still need to be done (like replying to emails or doing my volunteer work). Not particularly sensible (or even healthy...starting work at 3:00 AM isn't sustainable), but I've yet to find an alternative that works.

    That's the problem I've been having with Mark's system -- finding the balance between big projects with deadlines and all the other stuff that needs to be done. There's a huge difference between making *some* progress on a project, and making *enough* progress to meet the project's deadline.

  • Erik (and all) I've experienced exactly the situation you describe. I've cycled (and recycled) various systems and I still can't seem to hit the 'sweet spot'; that point where I feel I'm getting everything done that I need to while also ensuring sufficient progress on projects. I'm a Project Manager, it's a scary place to be with that in mind.

    I was introduced to AGILE as a project methodology some time back. It really resonated with me. It is effectively a way of developing software but creating a closed bucket of features in, say, a two week period. Anything not in that two week period is called 'backlog'. During the two week development, nothing can change - nothing added, but everything done. Does this all sound familiar? Effectively, DIT is a Personal Agile process. Of course there's more to AGILE than stated here but it's a valuable parallel. In AGILE as a project manager, you know whether or not the two week period is enough because you measure the 'velocity' of Developers' work. If you know how fast a developer is working and you know they're working on a closed list, you can also predict where they will be on the end date (after 2 weeks). If you calculate that they will be late, you can take steps to speed them up, perhaps introduce another developer etc. If on time, you can leave them be or simply tell them that their current work rate is fine.

    So the reason for this monologue? I think you need to know 1. Where do you need to be by a certain point in those projects and by when 2. What is your current velocity? (1) is easy if you know the project. (2) for me is the Holy Grail; I'm just waiting for Mark to read this, have an 'aha' moment and do what he does better than all of us; deliver a toolset/approach which does it!
