  • The degree of granularity is a question of how things stand out to you.

    Initially, it might make sense to list something "process inbox".

    Tackle that for however long your intuition suggests. During that task you might find yourself explicitly noticing specific pieces of paperwork standing out.

    So you might enter them separately: "Paper A", "Paper B"

    So after first working on "Process Inbox" you might end up with entries:

    Paper A
    Paper B
    Paper C
    Process Inbox

    The degree of granularity will finds it own balance as you practice and your ability to let things stand out grows. Let go a bit of your typical expectations and see what happens.

  • "where does that piece of paper reside until I "autofocus" on it?"

    Erik, you might like to try a system that I have used for some 12 months now of having a special "paper" tray on my desk. Like Alan, I like to keep my In-box empty and I find that there is often no natural file for paper related to a task in AF.

    For such papers, I use "letter files" (rather stiff plastic sleeves open on two sides). Once the task is entered into AF, I slip the paper into the letter file and place it at the bottom of the "paper" tray. That way, the papers are piled in the tray in exactly the same order as they appear in AF and they are easy to find when they stand out for attention in AF.

    At this present moment, I have eleven such temporary files in my "paper" tray.

  • Hi Erik

    Here is the link to an image of the letter files I referred to:
    https://orders.stationerysupplies.com.au/stock_images600/105601109.JPG
    A key advantage of these files is that they are open at the top and at the right and so it is very easy to slip a piece of paper in and out of them. Since they are transparent, you can see at a glance exactly what is in them.

    If you need to, you can pick up the whole pile of files and spread them like a deck of cards to find a required item. And if your supply comes in assorted colours, it can be even easier to find a particular item since you tend to remember what colour you use for a certain item. (Mostly I prefer clear plastic files since they are more transparent and they make it easier to see what is inside.)

    Having all your papers in these plastic files makes it very easy to slip new items in at the bottom of the pile so that the items stand in exactly the same order as thy stand in your AF list. If you care to use this strategy of slipping things in at the bottom, you will find that when your task comes up in AF, the related file is usually at or very near the top of the stack.

    One further thing I find helpful is that when I forget to re-enter an unfinished task in AF, the stray paper rises to the top of the stack and I am reminded to deal appropriately with it.

    Often an AF task will have 3 or 4 different pieces of paper, perhaps of different shapes and sizes, associated with it. These letter files are ideal for keeping such items together.
