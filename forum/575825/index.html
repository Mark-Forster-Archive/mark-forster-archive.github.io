Some questions for Mark and anyone with some input

  • I have read DIT three times now and have been very surprised by the great results I have been having!

    I have three questions that have been in the back of my mind since I started the processes, I was hoping for a reply from Mark or anyone that has some input.

    1. How do you handle Home tasks? I like to batch my tasks, but sometimes when I get home from work I am not in a home task kind of mood. One solution that I had was to forward these tasks in my task diary to Saturday when I know that I will have loads of free time.
    2. Projects. Can someone explain to me how DIT gives projects a “stake in the ground”? I believe that the solution to this would be to make sure I carry tasks forward in my task diary when writing my next days closed list, but I would like to hear some thoughts on this.
    3. And most importantly! In the task diary, do you cross out, check off, or highlight completed tasks?


    Thanks!

  • Hi Erik,

    Re 2. Although I find DIT a fantastic System I have really struggled with tracking my Projects, in the GTD sense of having a "stake in the ground". I am currently trialling having 2 sheets of paper stapled together that I re-circulate daily through my in-tray and 'will-do tray'. On the first sheet I list the Outcomes I want from the active projects that I have. On the second sheet I list 'Potential Organizational Projects'. I suppose the second sheet is similar to the GTD Someday/Maybe concept but I tend to keep this list as realistic as possible and in line with my existing Commitments. These are Pojects that I will do, in order.

    As Mark has commented, Organizational Projects (these do not have a deadline) are really best done one at a time if possible as taking on too many projects of this nature is likey to lead to Overwhelm. So this basically means that on my first sheet of paper I list the Outcomes I've set for myself that relate to Deadlined Projects, Continuous Projects and ONE Organizational Project. In fact I really like Mark's identification of different types of projects (see related articles on this site) as it seems logical and more realistic in terms of time available.

    I circulate this list through my Tray so that I get to read my Project Outcomes daily and list related actions in my Task Diary. On a related point I often do not write in Next Actions nowadays but just write '30 mins on X project' as I often find the Next Action concept a little stiffling.

    Re 3. I like to score out my completed task items with a red pen!

    Please do let me know if any of this does not seem clear, or if you have any ideas you could share.

  • Erik:

    Leon has done a very good job of answering your query, so I will confine myself to a few additional points:

    1) The work/home divide is always a difficult one, and comes up in many different forms for different people. My general advice is to have a separate Will Do list for each location. As someone who works from home, I therefore have one Will Do list which covers everything. However for personal tasks, I only ever put "chores" on the list, e.g. paying bills, mowing the lawn, cleaning the car. I don't include leisure activities - I don't want to spend my entire free time ticking items off a list - it's good to have some free-ranging time in which I simply do what I feel like doing.

    2) There are many possible ways of handling projects in DIT. It's important to pick the ways that fit the individual project and which suit you. I tend to recycle the project through my Task Diary on a daily basis when it is "live", i.e. put "Project X" as a task and do whatever needs doing on it. That sounds similar to what Leon is doing. Once the project goes quiet I will schedule a Review in my Task Diary for an appropriate period in the future, e.g. one week, one month.

    3) Personally I cross items out, which gives a great sense of satisfaction. It also gives a sense of achievement at the end of the day to see all those crossed out items!
