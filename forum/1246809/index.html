  • Alan:

    I'm working on this very problem right at the moment. My office has got into a state of disarray and I've had to "declare a backlog" on keeping the office tidy.

    What this means is that I've cleared two surfaces, and these are now the only places where I put anything once I've been working with it. I have a recurring task "Tidy Office" which refers only to these two surfaces (for the moment). Every time I do this task I make sure that I put everything on these surfaces back into their proper place (or allocate a proper place if it's a new item).

    At the same time I swept all the other stuff which needed tidying into a heap on the floor. I have another recurring task "Tidy Office Backlog". When I do this task, I work on the heap for as long as I feel like it. As a result it's quite quickly getting smaller.

    Once I've finished the heap, the "Tidy Office Backlog" task will be discontinued and the "Tidy Office" task will include the whole office.

    If you can't keep up with the new stuff, then you need to revise your system for dealing with new stuff. Are you clearing it often enough? Has everything got a place to go? Why is there so much? etc.

  • I don't like dumping all the backlog into one big pile. Yes, that's how GTD does it, but that doesn't mean it's right.

    Even though the backlog pile isn't perfectly organized, it still has some organization, and it's one you're used to. By dumping everything into one pile you lose that. It goes from "probably somewhere on the third shelf" to "somewhere in the pile that covers half the office".

    One of Spider Robinson's Callahan books talks about packing for a move. Start in one corner and pack. That's faster and less stressful than trying to purge and organize during the rushed side of the move, and you can let friends do it. At the new place, if you need something from a box that hasn't been emptied yet, you'll probably remember where it was in the old house, and hence which box it's in.

    GTD anticipates two or three full days organizing the big pile it creates. I don't have that much uninterrupted time, and even if I did I'd be making very bad decisions by the end of it.

    I like boxes with "throw out on" dates, but not as general dumping grounds. I evaluate each thing as it goes in. Receipts for small things get filed by month, and I throw them out after a year. Stuff I can't decide about, or that the kids aren't ready to give up, I label "review after". Often sitting in a box for a year is proof I don't need it any more, but sometimes seeing it again makes me smile so much that I decide to keep it.

  • I find there's a balance between time spent filing and time spent looking.

    I have a resistance to filing. If it's too time-consuming I end up with a useless pile. Also, filing breaks my train of thought when doing other things.

    The big picture is necessary. It makes no sense spending hours filing papers in the exact right place if you rarely need them. Likewise, if you often need to get pages from a file you want to find them quickly.

    I use the "half-inch" rule when filing.

    When the "to file" pile is over half an inch, "Filing" is added as a task in AF. I'm thinking of putting it on maintenance / automatic repeat.

    If a file is less than half an inch thick, I don't bother sorting the papers within it.

    If the file is larger, I divide it into smaller files (by year usually works well, or by 5 years). The only time I bother to sort finer than that is at year-end, When I have all 12 statements, it only takes a minute or two to put them in order and clip.

    Half an inch seems the magic number for most files. Much larger and it gets frustrating to search through. Much smaller and maintenance takes more time than searching.
