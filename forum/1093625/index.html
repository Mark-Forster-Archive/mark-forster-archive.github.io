  • I agree with Alan to skip re-writing of existing tasks on a list. I think if you have captured a task either do it or dismiss it, don't re-write it.

    I think if we added up all the re-writing time and used it to actually do something we would be much better off in the long run. The more time we spend managing our time i.e. working in our systems, the less time we spend actually doing. As we have discussed, there is a point where the TM system itself actually becomes a source of procrastination as we make it more complex, cumbersome and time consuming.

    I can't see an arbitrary limit on the list being productive, because you need to know the entirety of what you are dealing with. I loose productivity if I have to use memory to identify what needs to be done. Paper does this so efficiently.

    One technique I use is not to dismiss items during the day. During the day I only add items so I don't forget them. I use my mind to identify what has to be done and then at the end of the day, I do a daily review and remove items no longer needing to be done. This keeps me in doing mode during the day and I find I am more productive.

    Alan wrote: <<<What makes you guys think "standing out" is governed by "the wise part of ourself"?>>>

    When DWM is working well for me (ie. I feel at the end of the day that it was a day well spent, I made progress on my most important work while escaping burial by the odds & ends), I do feel that the "standing out" process works quite well, it engages intellect & intuition and helps me to focus. That combination of intellect & intuition, that feeling of being "in the zone", working the details effectively while still seeing the big picture of where it is all going and why, not being overwhelmed or pulled apart but rather feeling calm and energetic -- that seems to be the "wisest part of ourself" that David Allen refers to.

    And it usually DOES work well for me, unless I am sick. (Which unfortunately is pretty often). So I have these phases where I work DWM and feel on top of things, but then get sick, fall behind, get overwhelmed, lose focus, and slowly try to get back on top of things and keep stuff from going over the waterfall that really shouldn't. And then gradually I start to get back into that "zone" where I am making good decisions and things are clicking along. And all along the way, I keep trying to reduce my commitments so that the "recovery time" after a setback is shorter and less frustrating, and the time "in the zone" is maximized. The proportion of time spent "in the DWM zone" vs "overwhelmed and scattered" is still not high enough to make me happy, but it's higher than it was with AF and systematic improvement is more integrally part of the system, since DWM does such a great job of measuring your total workload and warning you when you've taken on too much.
