tickler/sdmb/waiting/dismissed/calendar

  • I don't recall the timeline but as I was re-vamping my desk around the time I started SF I started a tickler file for the first time. As it's for personal items only, I felt daily/43 folders was overkill. I went with weekly, then monthly. I realized somewhere in there if items in there are date specific, they are/should be/could be also on my calendar. If they're not date specific, I'm choosing an arbitrary time in the future that they'll re-surface. I'm finding that all those arbitrary things are getting pushed forward each time I encounter them. I'm finding this isn't working for me.

    What is working for me is using OmniFocus as the home for (as Jupiter put it) my non-actionable items. I have a 'dismissed from SF' list/project in there and move items there from my paper book as necessary. (I haven't moved anything back yet.) I'm thinking that since that's working for me, what I really need for those physical items that I want to consider 'sometime in the future' is one (or more, depending on how much stuff I have) holding file with it's contents in OF. Then it's not one more place to look.

    And for date specific things, because I know that most of the stuff in the tickler ISN'T date specific things and I haven't kept up with it religiously, it's not a trusted part of my system. Anything date specific should be on my calendar and again, I just need ONE file to keep any physical, date specific thing in, which are referenced on the calendar.

    Right now my SDMB is in another electronic document. I plan to integrate it into OF if this set up continues to work for me for the next month or two. It's more the 'wish' type things or 'when I go to ______ be sure to _______' type of things. So there's no hurry. But I think it would be better in the long run to have less places to look.

    Does that all make sense and seem to be a good direction?

    I didn't get any of this to work until I allowed paper things to live in their own parallel universe. My 43-folder tickler file has been functioning nicely for most of a year now, and I treat it as part of my calendar.

    At one point, I experimented with all-paper storage. I thought printing out my computer stuff into a paper system would be easier than entering all my paper into the computer. I soon discovered that most of my computer stuff could only be handled at the computer. Printing it all out, only to bring the paper back to the computer later on, grew increasingly silly.

    The policy I've since settled on is: things that originate in the computer stay in the computer, and things that originate on paper stay on paper. I only convert from one to the other if there is a specific, compelling reason to do so for that item. The major such reasons are searchability (easy retrieval later on inside the computer) and portability (printing out reports/lists to take somewhere).

    Now that I'm in SF, I jot down references to computer files/emails/bookmarks as needed. For calendar & reminders, I am still happy with my dual computer/paper systems.

  • The potential pitfall associated with using either a 43-folders-style tickler file or a calendar is the same: you might forget to check it in time.

    Please realize that we were discussing tricks or hacks for remembering to check the next day's folder. We weren't suggesting that it was the best place to store car keys or misc. paperwork. We could've suggested putting a push-pin on the next date on the calendar, and then hanging the car keys on that pin, for those who struggle with remembering to check the calendar.

    Putting theater tickets into the proper day's folder is a good way to avoid losing them. They could also be put into the previous day's folder, to serve as a reminder of the event itself, if you're likely to forget checking your calendar but are better at remembering to process your tickler folder each day.

    I see no reason for cluttering up my calendar with simple reminders of tasks to be completed. I reserve it for fixed-time appointments and all-day or multi-day events. Anything fluid or vague in terms of date & time can be better handled with a combination of tickler and to-do (SF) list, IMHO.

    Another great thing about using a tickler reminder, instead of overusing the calendar, is that each reminder is a separate sheet of paper or index card. For recurring tasks, I add notes or log my activities on the reminder sheet itself. I could also scribble all sorts of notes onto a paper calendar, but once the month is gone, those notes are lost. My paper reminders remain, however. So I know, for example, when I actually last changed my furnace filter (Dec, plan was Nov), because it's logged on the sheet that is still in my tickler file to remind me to change it again in May.

    I also use my tickler file for coupons. Previously, I had a big box for these. I struggled with trying to figure out the best way to deal with coupons. Since they all have expiration dates, I now put them into the appropriate month or day folder. I don't often use coupons, but I can quickly look at the next month folder, or the next few days' folders, to see if there are any useful ones. When I process today's folder, I look at any coupons that have landed there. If a coupon is expiring today, I might carry it around or just throw it away. If the expiration is a few days off, I'll either carry it around or move it to the expiration day's folder. It may not be the best solution, but at least now I don't have a box full of mostly-expired coupons!

    So I think there are several valid uses for a tickler file, other than auxiliary storage for items related to calendar entries.
