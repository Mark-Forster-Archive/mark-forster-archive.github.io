  • Something standing out and being motivated to do something are two entirely different things to me.

    Things need help getting done. AF helps me discern how I can help in that process. When something stands out, I start on it.

    "motivation, in psychology, the intention of achieving a goal, leading to goal-directed behavior."

    from http://www.answers.com/motivation under the _Columbia Encyclopedia_ entry.

    When something stands out, it does not mean I have the intent of achieving a goal. It is that very intention that paradoxically undermines my ability to help things get done. By listing the many things *I* believe need to be done, AF allows those things come to the surface that I might have never touched had I followed a goal-oriented approached. Likewise, some of my most important goals just get dismissed.

    AF helps me help things get done. Sometimes those things are what I hoped to get done as goals in the first place, but the approach is not goal driven. Rather than saying AF motivates me, I would say it removes resistance.

    By trusting AF and not worrying about results, I am more helpful.

    In the dishes example:

    It gets put on the list.
    It may eventually stand out (I don't want to do them, nor am I dreading it; it simply is the next thing to help get done).
    If it stands out, I start.
    Somewhere along the way I sometimes find myself wanting to do the dishes.

    Thankfully, that last part is unnecessary to the whole process but is great when it happens especially the sooner.
