Question for those who use only one list

  • Mark and others who use only one list, or who have only one major context, do you go about your day with literally no sense of "working hours" at all? Might you sit down at your desk at 8.30am on a Monday morning, ready to tackle some of the tasks that actually pay your mortgage, and then find that what was "standing out" was to get up and spend all morning cleaning out the garage instead? This isn't a criticism, I'm just interested. In my early days using AF (very positive so far), I'm definitely finding myself "suppressing" certain tasks in my mind after they have "stood out", because they're not work tasks, and it's "work time". It would seem very, very strange to me not to do this.

    (Apologies if I'm missing something obvious here. I've been through the FAQ thread.)

  • I think people are handling this in a number of ways...

    I have one book but I'm working from one end for work tasks and the other for personal tasks... so I consciously favor the "work" tasks during "normal" working hours by starting from that end of the book. However, if none of the "work" tasks stand out for me, I will flip to the other end of the book and see if any of my "personal" tasks grab me. Usually one does, and it will be a small one - like starting a load of laundry or something like that.* When I'm done working on that task, I come back to my book again starting on the work end - usually at this point I'll feel ready to tackle one of the work tasks again. Repeat throughout the workday, and what happens is that I spend much more time on "work" tasks than on "personal" ones - the "personal" stuff becomes my breaks from the "work".

    In the evening/on weekends, I do the reverse - start from the "personal" end of the book and do "work" stuff if the "personal" doesn't stand out for me.

    *Other days, like today, I end up spending an hour and a half actually finishing a big project. But my daughter's room has lovely new curtains now!

  • Hi Ludlow

    There is a lot of "accepted wisdom" that recommends defiining work time and work space for home workers, so that they do not overlap. I had often tried to do that, and felt I ought to do that, but found that my home items were my biggest procrastinators during work hours and that I was drawn to work tasks during "home" time.

    I started AF with one mixed list with no preconceptions about what to do. The first item that stood out was "make bed". Seemed a bizarre choice but I did it. I immediately realised that that one item had quite a significant impact, in that when the bed was made the bedroom carried the sense of being a sanctuary. That was an interesting revelation so I carried on - AF quickly identified a number of things that I unknowingly mentally associated with tasks - and over the weeks those tasks started to settle down into a pattern. Strangely that pattern left me doing primarily work style tasks in what I would consider work time, and domestic tasks at evenings and weekends. What I could never do myself (separating work/home), AF achieved effortlessly.

    My recommendation would be that you don't suppress what you feel is standing out because "it seems to be the wrong time". Let AF identify why it is important to do those tasks at that particular time. It is the extent to which we are prepared to listen to our subconscious that is the extent to which AF will work for us. You will know instinctively when to do certain things - if the choices seem odd go with them - there will be a reason ......

  • Ludlow:

    The rule you quote is aimed at the classic Home v. Work situation which about 95% of office-bound employees find themselves in. It is as if they live in two distinct worlds in which there is very little overlap - perhaps just the occasional task like "Call my bank" at work, and "Finish report by deadline tomorrow" at home.

    In that situation it's much more efficient to have two separate lists. You don't want to be scanning loads of irrelevant home tasks while at work, or loads of irrelevant work tasks while at home.

    However the situation becomes much greyer where there is a significant overlap between Home and Office tasks. In that case it can be quite difficult to judge whether it's more efficient to use one or two lists. Ultimately one can only decide by trying out both, and seeing which works best.

    My point is that, whichever you choose, you should work your list(s) according to the rules about dismissing tasks.

    I hope that has made it clearer.
