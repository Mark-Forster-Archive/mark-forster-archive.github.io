Multi-cause procrastination

  • This is a sort of follow-up thread to http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/1105469 ('Does anyone have any advice for overcoming serious procrastination?') which I have found very interesting reading, but is getting a bit longer and my thoughts were slightly tangential.

    It was pointed out in that thread that different people procrastinate for different reasons. I find (and I'm sure I'm not alone in this) that I myself procrastinate for different reasons, depending on the situation. This can make it much harder to tackle the procrastination, since at any given point I need to have enough self-awareness to know what is causing me to procrastinate before I can do anything about it.

    For me these are the things I have identified:

    - I typically start procrastinating by worrying that what I produce won't be good enough (a sort of 'unproductive perfectionism')
    - next, if the work is being done for someone else, I start to feel guilty/ashamed/embarrassed that I've still not got it done, and procrastinate to avoid these feelings
    - then part of me starts to feel resentful about these feelings, and by extension my job, and it becomes a sort of 'demand-resistance' procrastination about anything I'm expected to do
    - eventually when the pressure of the backlog of work gets too much I try to start on it, but at that point start to procrastinate because I am feeling completely overwhelmed.

    In addition to these main points there can be other factors:

    - tiredness (I am a fairly new Dad and am also trying to train myself in web design in the evenings)
    - demotivation, brought on by questioning if my work is really achieving anything
    - lack of focus - distraction by non-work-related things, either worries about house, family, money etc or excitement about something positive happening
    - resistance to a task which I would always find inherently unpleasant, for whatever reason (e.g. for me, anything involving confrontation)

    In the past I've always tried to narrow it down to one simple thing - (e.g. *this* is why I *really* procrastinate) and of course very quickly end up procrastinating again for a different reason, and end up feeling depressed and hopeless that I could change...

    Sorry for the rant - just interested to know really if this experience resonated with anyone else?

  • Daniel,

    Either beg, buy, borrow or steal a copy of the book I mentioned in the previous thread, Self -Discipline in 10 days, by Theodore Bryant. It covers all the various messages we send ourselves that result in self-defeating behaviour and attitudes.
    You can recognise the different reasons you procrastinate, they are simply different messages that one part of you is sending the other, informing you that you can't /won't / shouldn't have to / do whatever it is you're procrastinating about. Once you are consciously aware of that then you can "re-programme" your subconscious by sending it conflicting messages.

    It also really helps to write down the negative thought as it flits through your mind, seeing it down on paper makes you realise that it's just a thought, not even real. Then you write a truer and more positive version, it all helps to change your attitude and then in time your behaviour changes too.

    here are some examples from my notebook:

    oh no I'm never going to get all this lesson done

    changed to -

    It can be done over two days, you can make a start now and see how you do. Just ten minutes is all.

    this trip to the dentist is going to be painful.

    changed to -

    the dentist is skilled and will give you enough anesthetic so you don't feel much. You'll be uncomfortable but nothing else.

    ( as it turned out I didn't need treatment so I was getting myself in a panic for nothing!)
