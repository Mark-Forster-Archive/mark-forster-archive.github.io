  • I do not have Andreas' talent with flow charts/animations, but here's what my DWM system looks like.

    The setup:
    I have a regular 5"x7" lined notebook. I use one sheet (front and back) for each date. This means that I can rip pages out as all the tasks on them are completed and gives me enough room for everything. If need be, I can resort to writing two columns on each page.
    The first page in the book is today's (I call it Page D). Tasks on this page that I don't get to today will be eliminated from the list automatically.
    I have two bookmarks (really colored paperclips): one for today + 7 (30 March - I call it Page W) and one for today + 1 month (23 April - I call it Page M). There has been a LOT of debate about the definition of "month" - some people use 30 days, some people use 28. I have chosen to go with calendar month, which I think is what Mark was doing as well. These are the points of entry for all tasks, the open lists for the day: new tasks go on page M; re-entered tasks go on page W. All pages between page D and page W are permanently closed lists and will never be added to again.

    The process:
    As I was composing this post I thought of a handful of new tasks, so I opened my book to page M and jotted down
    - wash neb parts
    - pack up paula (my daughter's nebulizer)
    - unload dishwasher
    Yes, these are tasks that I need to get to before 1:40 this afternoon but they are NEW so they go on page M.

    Now let's pretend I've started working on my DWM list.
    - I open my book to where I left off last night, which is indicated by a paperclip on the side of the notebook (the bookmark ones are on the top).
    - I read through the list and when a tasks stands out as "ready to be done" I will do it.
    - I decide that the "guest room" task is ready to be done, so I go and pull the sheets off of the guest bed and put them into the laundry in preparation for houseguests this weekend. I then re-enter it on page W because it's not completed yet.
    - As I'm doing this, I remember that I need to talk to my parents about when they're coming to visit us this summer, so I add that task on page M.
    - I continue reading through my list starting from the original "guest room" entry, waiting for another task to stand out.
    - I make it to page M without any tasks standing out and so I flip back to the beginning of the list (page D). These are the tasks that are going over the edge if I don't work on them today. I work on transcribing an interview but don't complete it. It is saved! Or re-entered on page W, at least.
    - I continue reading through my list starting from the original "transcribe interview" entry that I worked on. The next task that stands out is "750 words" and it's the last one on a page that falls between page D and page W - a closed list than can never be added to again. I work on it, re-enter it on page W, and RIP OUT THE PAGE! (This is where my implementation may differ from others'; I don't keep completed pages around.)

    And I work round and round like that all day. New tasks go on page M, re-entered tasks go on page W. For today, all other lists are closed. Pages between D and W that get completed can be removed.

    At the end of the day, I rip out today's page whether all the tasks are completed or not and advance my bookmarks by one day. Depending on what the following day looks like, sometimes I use Mark's current initiative modification (see here: http://www.markforster.net/blog/2010/2/10/a-couple-of-options-for-dwm.html) or I sometimes don't. Working in this way I usually get 2-3 days ahead during the week which is good because my list gets very little attention during weekends.

    I hope this makes sense & is helpful to you!
