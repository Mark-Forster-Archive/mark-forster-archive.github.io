  • Norman makes a good point - a long list can be overwhelming. I am coming to realise though that there seems to be two distinct ways of viewing those lists and that that impacts on people's views and management of AF, whether 1 or 2.

    I think the difference is whether we view the list as "things to be done" or "tasks, ideas and possibilities". If we see the list as "things to be done - which is of course a very practical and logical approach, then the longer the list the greater the sense of overwhelm. If however the list is more of a collecting point - a memory aid for everything, then the length of the list becomes to a degree irrelevant (although method of working it may become more of an issue).

    I definitely fall into the latter category. I "know" I have things to do but if they are not defined on a list, no matter how long that list may be, I have a constant sense of unease. I am far, far less stressed by the thought that I have 2,394 things to do which are a,b,c, d,etc than "I have so much to do". Clearly others would be freaked out by that concept and no doubt the thought of my 60+ active pages in AF1 would have had them running screaming for the hills.

    The great thing about this forum is that it enables all of the different approaches to looked at, perhaps even identified for the first time, and solutions found. What works for one will not work for everybody but you can bet that it will definitely work for more than just the one!

  • Hi Rainer

    That's an interesting question and my instinctive reaction is that there should not necessarily be any difference between the tasks put into AF1 or AF2. In fact I have noticed that my tasks are entered differently (in that I do not need to add so many duplicates for routine items within AF2) but basically the type of item is consistent. AF is designed to work well with mind dump type lists in that there is no need to grade tasks but it seems that a lot of people a finding that they are feeling a need to grade within AF2 more than with AF1.

    My feeling is that it is not so much the list itself but the perception of a list and how it "should" work. I love the fact that everything I need to do, from a tax return to entering a telephone number into my diary, can be on one list. That is what is important to me. I love seeing progress, but to me that is best expressed as a percentage of tasks done; I can see that I am being productive in the fact that I am doing things which I have decided to do (by putting them on the list). If the perception of a to do list is more of a "this must be done and done quickly" list then that would create immense pressure the longer the list got.

    The only time I would use a more "traditional" to do list, would be say when I am working to very specific deadline, such as packing and preparing for a trip. Then I will do a very specific list which would be in effect a check list of total must do items. Does that help?
