  • Michael,

    I'm not sure that the " precise, peaceful, and elegant daily experience," is the equivalent of the 50,000 view. I think it is the effect of the total system.

    In GTD, the metaphor is artificial flight: you work up to the 50,000' Life Goals only after clearing your runway of basic, daily work. I never really got the hang of anything above the project level, and I suspect that the beautiful hierarchy of planning levels was a rather artificial construct, bolted on to a practical, intuitive system.

    With SF, the metaphor is a journey through a forest, following an unseen quarry. Your true goal may not be obvious, but it can be inferred from looking at what you have actually done. SF does not claim to help you soar above the forest, but does provide a handy machete to clear the undergrowth off your path.

  • I agree with both Will and Alan that the useful parts of GTD and SF are about how to manage your list of tasks, i.e. the minute-by-minute processing, rather than higher-level abstract considerations of goals, plans, reflections, reviews, etc., which most people will do only occasionally (once daily or less frequently). If you want a system that emphasizes these abstract activities, Franklin-Covey isn't bad. But when you get down to actually doing tasks, I would substitute Mark's SuperFocus for F-C's priority-based list method.

    If you just want to insert periodic reminders to do the "higher-altitude" stuff, I highly recommend using your 43-folders tickler file (one of the best "tricks" I ever learned from David Allen). If you get used to using this, it's a lot better than a [paper | electronic] calendar, because it can hold physical papers & cards, and because it's so simple to move the reminder in order to "do it tomorrow" when you know you haven't the time or energy for a periodic review or planning session today. Each reminder card or sheet of paper can also be used to log the activity; so, when you come to it, you can see instantly when the last time was that you actually did it.

  • I'm actually rather puzzled why no one else thinks SF can effectively address these high-level issues.

    Addressing these questions would fall under the category of "discretionary time", I think (unless you specifically delegate such activities to regularly scheduled appointments with your spouse or manager or mentor or pastor or psychotherapist or whatever to work on it). :-)

    And SF is designed to handle everything in your discretionary time.

    So my conclusion is SF can handle this kind of thing quite well.

    And that conclusion has been validated by experience in using the system, as I wrote recently here: http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/1443971#post1447434

    It's easy to trigger this kind of high-level reflection on the direction of one's life by entering items in the SF list such as:

    - Am I really doing what I want/should/need to be doing with my life?

    - What am I really trying to do? What is stopping me from doing it?

    - Read "How to make your dreams come true" and do the mind exercises therein

    - What are my 25-year goals? 5-year? 1-year? How am I tracking toward them? What is stopping me from achieving them?

    I have found that the more viscerally I phrase these items, the more effective they are. Sometimes when these kinds of tasks "stand out", I do some mind mapping and start connecting the dots in my life, writing down the things that really trouble me, the things that really seem to be working, etc., and then put any relevant things that need more follow-through back into SF.

    In summary, I suppose you could say I treat this high-level reflection like any other ongoing project -- I have at least one item in SF that prompts me to review the project and see how it's going overall, look for gaps, see how we're tracking towards milestones, etc.; and I also enter anything that can be actioned now, whether it's simple reflection on a particular topic or issue, or reading a book, or discussing something with my wife, or whatever.

  • I don't think I'm saying anything too different than what's been said, but I feel like I have to say it anyway.

    Many people "do GTD" without doing the reviews, which means they stay at the runway/10,000ft level and stay busy "doing". I see this as the place where SF can serve as the method for best (for me) organizing my doing and keep me moving forward. If I write down ALL my thoughts in SF, there will naturally be some higher level thoughts that will get encouraged and move forward with something in the present or dismissed. If you're reviewing your dismissed items in SF, then you will have to reflect on these things and figure out what to do with them. SF just doesn't specify what is to be done with them. That's up to you.

    I think that just as many people 'do GTD' without doing anything at higher levels, many people do SF like that as well. Some people like/need a system that helps them/encourages them to reflect on the bigger picture (GTD altitudes/FC) some do it naturally, some don't do it at all.

    I think the simplicity of SF lets it be a stand-alone 'doing' system or a component in any one of many larger systems that include bigger picture stuff. It's up to the individual to figure out their need.

  • AF/SF work great for high level planning, without needing a weekend retreat.

    One option is to add "High level planning" to the list as a task. It's a long one, a good candidate for the little-and-often approach.

    Or throw all your possible goals at the list and let it help you decide. Mark has written at length about reviewing tasks that keep getting dismissed. First you investigate why that task keeps getting dismissed, then you take appropriate action. If it still keeps getting dismissed, there's another reason that must be addressed.

    The dismissal part of AF/SF is the key here. Anything that shouldn't be a true goal gets removed. All that remain are goals you will make progress on.

    Under Covey and GTD, undone tasks and goals hang around until the next high-level review, not getting done and clogging up the page. GTD tells you to unblock them by deciding a next action, which often works, but doesn't help if the real problem is it's not a good goal. Covey suggests you put one task for each goal on your list each week, but doesn't say what to do if the same task isn't done each week.

    Michael: I like your Flow State list. It describes what I feel during and after a productive day, and looks like it will help me turn an unproductive moment into a productive one, and help those moments add up to days.
