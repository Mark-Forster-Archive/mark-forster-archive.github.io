  • I have been using MArk's system for 2-3 years now using GED, DIT & AFs 1-4 and here is where I am up to

    I have found that the way I use AF4 varies precisely because the sheer quantity of tasks I have to keep moving can be overwhelming. However a main benefit of af4 is that nothing gets missed because I write down those things that could be done. Because I keep all my pages in an archive file, I know I can go back and recapture possibilities from before.

    I took Mark's concept of "Daily Tasks" which was in one of the books, and have changed it to 48 hour tasks - tasks that I know need done regularly so that my work keeps flowing. To myself I call this "sweeping the runway" - concorde sadly crashed because someone did not make sure the runway was swept and eventually it caught up with them. I keep this separate because it is 28 tasks that I do not want to manually enter into AF4. I may time limit the length of time to do my 48 hour tasks in which case any left undone go into the active AF4 list

    Secondly I run through AF 4. I have three ways I do this: standard; with highlighting; with closed lists:
    When time is very tight I go through the AF 4 list and create a closed list on the right hand side of my final page. I focus on these tasks using the timing system I learnt from Mark until time runs out or I think I have done enough then I put them to the end of the active list. I use a computer timer or online timer to keep me on track.

    When I feel the need to rationally plan at the beginning of the day, I may go through my AF4 list with a highlighter underlining anything that has been slipping using standard AF4 and cycle through those once. This means that I focus on the tasks and don't reflect on the whole list in that cycle. This can be a relief on occasion!

    Other times, like today I do AF4 as standard which includes reading this forum.

    Finally, if I find a lot of tasks relating to one theme I may turn it into a project. A project goes in CAPITALS in my AF4 system and then I have a separate section of my notebook for projects. When running through AF4 I treat a project as a normal entry. When I reach a project I may use any of the approaches above. I may also schedule time to work on particular projects.

    Finally, in my notebook I have a third section for notes.

    When I have completed a page I do not chuck it but file it as often there is useful info there.

    I like the fact that Mark's approach is experimental. It encourages us to work it out as we go along and to keep adjusting. He is less of a parent telling all those who need to learn : "Do it this way, dummy" but he is more of a coach suggesting things for us to use and work on.
