Going backwards through the pages

  • Has anyone experimented with going BACKWARDS through the pages instead forward - that means, you start on your last page, when nothing more stands out, you go to the page before etc. until you arrive at the first open page, after which you return to the last page?

    I don't propose this as an alternative rule, I simply want to know whether this was tried out by anyone (maybe in the beta test phase) and which difference it makes.

    I was thinking about the fact that the most recent tasks tend to be on the last pages, and that some things get irrelevant with time, etc.

    I was even thinking about testing a more complex rule, for example, to return to the last page every time you dismiss a page while going backwards. What would this mean? Or going back to the end every time you start a new page, and then again working backwards.

    Has anyone TESTED (!) this?

  • I promised to report.

    So...

    Simply going in reverse order changes actually nothing. It seemed to be better at first look, because at that time I had some fresh but urgent tasks to do, which where on the last pages, of course, so I started where the action was and got a lot done. But... Soon you're on page 9 again, while things shelve up on page 23, 24... so you either have to jump out of the cycle or work yourself through 8 more pages (backwards) before going back to the last page.

    Therefore (and inspired by Greenchutneys SMALL AF-LIST-concept), I will - from today on - test the following tweek:

    Start on the last CLOSED page. That's the page before the last one. Act on it as usual (skimming for items that stand out etc.). If you leave that page (either by working on at least 1 item or dismissing the page), you go to the page before - that is, backwards. While working, the AF list grows longer by adding new items or follow up items.

    And now the rule: WHENEVER YOU CLOSE A PAGE (and start the next on), YOU JUMP BACK TO THAT FRESHLY CLOSED PAGE. And continue as described above.

    The reasoning behind is:

    - On the newest pages are a lot of items that are not yet ripe (so they won't stand out) plus items that may be urgent - and these one you get managed in time this way.

    - On the older pages are only items left that have had their chance to get done several times, so maybe they end up as irrelevant anyway - no big deal if they get dismissed a bit later.

    - If your current situation is rather hectic, your list will grow faster: Then you return more often to the LCP (last closed page) and keep your head up. When things get normal or calm, you'll have the time to dive deeper into the backlog. This way, the system adapts to your workload by itself and without the necessity (I hope) for additional crutches like index cards etc.

    Again: This is an experiment. I'll report again.
