AF for tasks that can't be done little & often

  • I have tasks that are high-resistance that aren't conducive to little and often. I will give you the specifics, but I know there are quite a few tasks that are similar. Wondering what Mark and others have to say.

    My goal for this year was to spend 1/2 hour of "special time" a week with each of my children. I have this time scheduled. One would think I wouldn't resist it, but I do. I let me children choose what they'd like to do during this time, so I know I may be spending half an hour playing Candyland. Ugh. LOL I often try to get the kids to do something with me that I enjoy doing or need to do, but it's usually to no avail. I've tried changing the way I think about this time (e.g., how blessed I am that my children want to spend time with me), but usually I resist and avoid the time anyway.

    I just added each child's special time to my AF list two days ago. The kids aren't happy. LOL I think it may very well break down my resistance. BUT I can't very well "Just get the board game out" with my kids. They will mutiny! It's true that I could do less than 30 minutes, but not much or again the kids will complain. It seems to me that there are other tasks like this: all or nothing.

    I am open to suggestions on how to manage these times with my kids that in my heart of hearts I really DO want to do. But the way it's structured now, I feel much resistance to it. I've skipped it twice on my AF list now and feel a lot of guilt.

  • Hi Mel

    One of things I'm finding is that it is the scheduling aspect of tasks that can cause me an issue. I have found tasks that I want to do, that I enjoy doing, having resistance simply because of the fact that I have scheduled "a time to do them". Obviously I don't know what ages your children are or how practical it would be but could you perhaps make a game of it that they will have their time each week but that the time will just "pop up" at varying times during the week?

    There will be a reason and as it is clearly not the task itself just take some time to look at the other factors (time of scheduling, lack of control as the children choose the activity, wanting to be doing something else at that particular time etc.)

    Congratulations by the way on readjusting your AF system - I tried playing with different things but they all increased resistance in some way or other. I think it is a healthy part of the learning curve to try other things - it makes us see how good the system really is - and that sense of relief on homecoming is pretty cool!

  • Hi Mel, I can totally understand your struggles here! We also have six children, including one set of twin boys. We learned pretty quickly that this particular set of twin boys really likes doing everything together -- they feel like their brother is missing out if I do something with them just 1-on-1. So, I usually (but not always) have that "special time" with them together.

    And that led to mixing up the special time quite a bit for everyone else, too, so that sometimes it's 1-on-1, and sometimes there's two or three of them. Sometimes it's just going for a drive to the hardware store or something. We can talk or listen to an interesting podcast and talk about it (Discovery Channel has some great audio podcasts). Or maybe it's just sit and read with the 4-year-old for 10 minutes during a break from work (when I'm working from home). Or showing the 8-year-old how to rotate the sprinkler so all the grass gets covered. Or maybe playing a board game (but the twins like the complex ones, like Settlers of Catan, so it's pretty hard fitting it in sometimes!). Or maybe just going for a short walk together.

    So, I do have "spend time with X" on my list, but I leave it open-ended -- maybe we'll do something like I listed above, which is usually somewhat spontaneous. But maybe we'll block out some time on the weekend to do something that takes more time. And maybe we'll include some of the others, and maybe even my wife. :-) (She spends most of the day with them, so it's not like she needs to work more opportunities into her schedule...)

    This arrangement seems to work OK. I still wish I had more time for them all. But at least the feeling of being "connected" with each one individually is maintained.

    One more thing -- putting it on the AF list and keeping it somewhat spontaneous allows me to adjust to the changing needs of the children. Sometimes the four-year-old needs some extra doses of attention, several times during the week, or even several times (in small doses) during a single day. On the other hand, the 13-year-old is more independent, and as long as we have a chance for a long talk now and then (usually when we're taking a longer drive), she's OK. And sometimes it changes around, depending on circumstances. Using AF for this allows you to be guided by your intuition, rather than just following an arbitrary schedule.
