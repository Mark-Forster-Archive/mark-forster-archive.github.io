SFv3: "Play Tag" Hack

  • Problem:

    Frequently, I am not sure if I could proceed to the next page without dismissing the current page. Because I can't remember whether I have touched on any C1 item in this session.

    C2 does not have to be monitored at all since all the items in C2 must be struck through before turning to the next page. If there's anything unmarked, I simply can't move on. Period.

    C1, on the contrary, is a bit harder to track as I can't be confident if I have fulfilled the minimum requirement to keep this page active.

    After having crossed off trivial C1 item, the fact is easily forgotten if I stay on the page for a fair amount of time, dealing mostly with Big Rock C2 items.

    With a page that has many items already crossed off, worked-on-during-this-visit item is almost indistinguishable from the other marked off items from past sessions.

    A Hack:

    There may be several options to alleviate this problem, but I've found the following to be simple and motivating:

    Put two Post-Its on the current page. One is just a bookmark to show it's "current", and the other is a kind of tickler to remind you of this key question -- "Have you worked on C1?".

    * Put the "current" Post-It on, well of course, the current page.

    * You have one more Post-It on the same page, to remind that "you haven't worked on any item on C1 since you arrived at this page".

    * Once you address one item from C1, you peel off and move this Post-It to the next active page, while the other, i.e. the "current" bookmark, still left on.

    * Continue working on the current page until no more tasks feel ready to be done. Then you go to the next active page with the "current" Post-It. So the "current" tag usually follows one step behind, chasing after the "C1?" tag.

    * No matter how hard you worked on items from C2, as long as you haven't touched anything from C1, your "C1?" tag should stay on the current page. Then you will know, at a glance, that you can't move forward without having to dismiss the rest.

    * If you've dismissed the page, advance both marks together to the next active page.

    Tips:

    * You may use different colors for each bookmark; e.g. yellow for the "current" and pink for the "C1?". Or simply get by with one color, just writing the label on each. I personally prefer the latter, for I can't remember which color is which.

  • I've faced the same problem, and solved it in the following way:

    1. Whenever I start working on a task, the first thing I do is draw a dot beside it in the list. I can't remember who suggested this, but I find it very useful...by doing this, I'm effectively telling myself "this is what I'm going to do now". It's like I'm committing to the task.

    2. When I've finished working on a task, I cross out the task itself, but I don't join the crossing-out line with the dot -- I leave a small gap.

    3. Then, when I'm ready to leave the page, I join the crossing-out line with the dot, and also draw vertical lines connecting the dot with any completed tasks above or below -- just like Mark suggests in his instructions so you can quickly see which tasks haven't been crossed out yet.

    It sounds a bit awkward to write down like this, but I find it works brilliantly -- I can immedateiy see which tasks I've done on the current run-through of the page, because they have dots beside them that aren't joined to a horizontal or vertical line.

    WHEN I'M READY TO TURN THE PAGE, I go through and put vertical lines at one end of the horizontal ones so that as more and more tasks are achieved it turns into just 1 vertical line (as discussed in past posts since beginning of AF).

  • I just realized that KE's dot trick obviates the need for any flag or tag or bookmark! When you visit a page, you are required to choose one of two options: (1) pick a task, or (2) dismiss the page. If you choose to dismiss, you visit the next page. So as soon as you pick a task, you place the dot. There shouldn't be any lonely dots hanging out on any other page. You can take action on the chosen task immediately, or take a break. Either way, there will be exactly one page with one or more dots on it (that haven't been connected to lines). If you now close your notebook and open it some time later, you can quickly flip through all the active pages and look for a page that has one or more dots on it – that is the current page!

    The top green flag says 'Start'. I put this flag on the page I start my day with.
