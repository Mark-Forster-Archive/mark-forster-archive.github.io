DWM for the project-focused

  • I can see the appeal of DWM for general tasks. But what about those of us who are tightly project focused?

    I'm a journalist. I work on a rolling list of deadlined projects, most of which arise and are finished within a week or two. (Although I am right there with Douglas Adams when he said something to the effect of, "I love deadlines. I love the whooshing noise they make when they go past.")

    I might work on an article on and off for a couple of weeks, in bursts. When it's time to work on that project, it seems counter-intuitive to be riffling through a month's worth of pages to find the contacts I'd listed during my last burst. I just want a straightforward list of to-dos related to that project so I can crunch through them. I also tend to have lots of outstanding calls and emails waiting to be returned.

    I use Omnifocus, so I can see the possibility of flicking back-and-forth between project and list views. But when work is so highly project- and deadline-orientated, do the advantages of DWM fade? On face value, I should gel with GTD - only I don't. I'm looking for a sort of mash-up between the structured approach of GTD and the fluidity of the AF/DWM concepts, plus the "day's work" container of DIT.

    Actually, I'm really looking for a couple of weeks at the beach.

    Matt

  • The DWM system doesn't work by the calendar. The way this system works, you often end up clearing tasks out well before the system says they are due, so a task *written on* February 25 must be *worked* on *before* Feb25, which can easily be today if you choose.

    So *always* enter tasks on day +7 or +30 regardless of when they are due. If there is a special due date, write that date beside the task. Work on tasks regardless of what day they are written on. Select whatever you feel is important, urgent, or doable. So instead of putting your task on a certain date, write the due date beside the task as a reminder.

    A separate calendar is perfectly acceptable, but use the list to drive your work habits.

    GTD? Not really. The GTD system is Collect everything, Process everything, Organize everything. Mark Forster's method is Little and Often: do a little. do a little more. If you are stuck doing, process a little. If in doing you need to organize, organize a little.

    Until I started on my current incarnation using ListPro, I was concerned about tracking the next task for projects or big task, essential for the 'little and often' treatment.

    I suspect the OmniFocus and Excel users have something similar, but I now set up a project in ListPro, then put the various tasks required as sub-tasks. The parent project appears in my Closed List or just left where it currently resides (<7days or <28 days), then I can expand it and see right away what has already been done (checked + strikethrough) and what I fancy doing or must do next.

    And what is really nice is that each sub-task can have its own date in accordance with the DWM rules, but still be nested under the parent task project.

    I also use nesting for Daily, Weekly and Monthly Routines.

  • "I can see the prospect of a forward wave effect, where the first month goes beautifully but then you hit a mass of old undone tasks that slow or stop momentum (I realise that this shouldn't be the case, but I'm talking about me)."

    This kind of comment comes up a lot. I am just now hitting my first batch of tasks that have rolled forward and I've had a very interesting experience dealing with them.

    First I said, "wow, I haven't done anything AT ALL on this task, despite having 30 days to do so? That's pitiful"

    Then I looked at the two tasks. One made me say, "Well, that was stupid to put on the list in the first place--no wonder I didn't do it!"

    The other made me say, "Aw, I can't let that little fellah slip away, after sticking with me all this time. I'll just do that one little thing..."

    And, in that time, I did (or began) EVERY OTHER THING on the list. Wow, for me, that's fantastic.

    So, that 30 days mark, doesn't signify the crashing waterfall of undone tasks. It's a reminder to you to decide what's important and what's worth working on.
