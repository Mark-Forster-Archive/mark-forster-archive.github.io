Super Auto 'Doom' Focus Everything Tomorrow

  • In my estimation, SuperFocus does all other systems better. It lets you take charge, and bend it to your needs. I will attempt to demonstrate each case:

    Auto Focus 1: Make your task sizes, as you work on them, small, and your work pattern will be exactly that of AF1, entering the next stage of your task on the last page, But you can also add urgent new tasks on the spot, solving AF1's biggest flaw.
    AF2: With a little tweak, you can process each page by AF2 rules, bottom to top. Every time you work on something it goes to the bottom. AF2 broke down because it couldn't get through the list. Being restricted to one page, that's no longer a problem. Finish a page, move on, and add your new urgent tasks on the new active page.
    AF3: See AF2. Both urgent and old tasks get well covered by this method.
    AF4: This system works quite well, until your list gets really long as a couple people have observed. One huge open list and one huge closed list mask the beneficial effect of the closed list. In SuperFocus, *every page* is an open list and a closed list. You can effectively do AF4 on every page, though I'm not sure there's a reason to: SAF does things just fine.
    DWM: This is more tricky because SuperFocus lacks the dated dismissal. And in many ways DWM already has much of SuperFocus's character. To get dated task control in SF, you could start a new page every day (or just add a date after the newest task) and dismiss the oldest page after one month.
    Given that, here are SF's advances:
    - Only worry about current and last page, not 3 entry points.
    - Keep active tasks on the current page , easier to continue working.
    - All the active stuff is clearly separated on the right column. It's easy to keep these going by working them repeatedly. This is often better than the weekly focus DWM pushes.

    3T: While it wasn't intended to use the right-side to work on only 3 tasks at a time, you can do that if you wish. It fits the rules. But you aren't restricted to 3. If something comes up, you can work on it, and get back to your 3. You can switch modes, charge through a bunch of pages doing small things, and get back to what you were doing before.

    Get Everything Done: Work in repeated bursts of activity, on a variety of tasks. Good news: With SAF you have a constant supply of varied tasks to apply this method. I don't know too much about this one so I'll stop there.

    Do It Tomorrow: Did you notice when Mark trialed Super Auto Focus he announced what he wanted to achieve the next day? In doing this, he didn't plan ahead and customize his AF list. SAF just got those things done - and other stuff - without preplanning. But the rules let you plan stuff out if you like. Gather a list of things you want to do, add them to SAF, and do them AutoFocus style. When you've made good progress, go through other pages of SAF and work on stuff. You may carry a DIT task with you, and when that's done, pick another to take along.

    ---
    Above I attempted to prove that SuperFocus does everything better. Did I succeed?

    DWM2 (as I am using now) actually addresses those weaknesses of DWM, and actually I can work on DWM2 like SAF (sorta) by doing it "Ping Pong" style; that is, by alternating between my current page and the last pages.

    1) DWM2 has only 1 entry point: the end. Thus, like SAF, you only have to worry about your current page and the pages near the end.

    2) Because of the one entry point, all "active," especially unfinished, tasks, are at or near the end, and because of the highlighted character of unfinished tasks in DWM2, you are compelled to finish those tasks. Currently I have no unfinished tasks past 2 days ago.

    2) When you repeatedly work on the unfinished tasks, they keep on being added to the end. Which means they keep on being highlighted and worked on until they are done.

    And oh, DWM2 has one advantage over SAF: autonomy. SAF for me had the added overhead/stress/complexity/whatever of you having to decide whether to dismiss a page or not. In DWM2, it is time based and removes the decision from you, hence it is more autonomous.

  • Maybe each time you fill a new page you close an old page? This will keep inflow=outflow.

    That might be too strict. Sometimes we need to accept inflow that exceeds outflow, in the short term. Sometimes we write extra details, so new tasks use pages quickly. (If the quick phone call produces extra notes, I take them on whatever is handy. My Task book is handy and the notes won't get lost. Flipping to the back for a short note disrupts the conversation, and we all know what short notes often grow into.)

    Maybe dismiss one page every day. If you add new pages slowly (perhaps by writing big tasks rather than little ones), use half-pages or remove a page every other day.

    Each day, record the number of active pages. Each week, look at the record to see if it's growing or shrinking and why.

    I expect the number will grow at first, as we brainstorm and bring more of our lives into the system. It will also grow when we are busy and shrink when we pull ahead. It will grow if we subdivide tasks more finely or take more notes than usual.

    It will stay constant if we are staying even.

    Another option is to track the number of active tasks. That will take longer, avoid the problem with embedded notes, but not solve the problem with differing granularity.

    Yet another option is track total time committed. That will take even longer, and only work if we list all the subtasks in a project and estimate times accurately. I like time estimates when planning a day or week, but maybe not for this.
