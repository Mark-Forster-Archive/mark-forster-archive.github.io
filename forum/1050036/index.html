Resistance ahead

  • I have been having success with the little and often approach, but I have a snag that needs some help.

    Some of my ‘next actions’ cannot be broken down any more. For example: I have used AF to do all of the tasks leading up to painting the room, or grouting the floor. These next tasks cannot be done properly in a little and often manner, each is a commitment of at least a few hours to get to a stopping point.

    Now I am experiencing quite a bit of resistance due to the size of the tasks and it’s not something that I particularly want to do anyway. Other than the ‘just suck it up and do it’ approach, have any of you any advice on a better way to proceed for now and in the future?
    23 March 2010 at 12:09 | Tuck
  • Tuck:

    I would suggests that you use timed bursts here.

    Set an alarm for 5 minutes, work until the alarm goes off, then do something else for 5 minutes then reset the alarm for 10 minutes, then 15, 20 etc. Take 5 minutes break between each burst. You can do anything during the break except work on the project.

    It's important with this timed burst method, that you keep the bursts exact. Don't run over on either the work or rest phases.
    23 March 2010 at 13:27 | Mark Forster
  • <<Other than the ‘just suck it up and do it’ approach>>

    Actually, that's about the best approach. You can try Mark's timed bursts approach if you like, but I'm not sure it works for something like painting a room.

    Another of Mark's suggestions which is useful is the 'Just Get The File Out' approach (which conveniently is also, in accronym form, nearly synonymous with 'Just Get The F. On with it'). Rather than have 'paint the room' on your list, have 'put dust sheets out', or 'mix paint for room'. Anything which is a more 'do-able' first step. Then just trust that your natural 'completion urge' will take over at that point.

    Even if you consciously 'know' that you will do the whole job, telling yourself you'll just do the smaller first step (sometimes I even tell myself out loud), somehow fools part of your mind enough to get started.

    Failing that, you could always pay for a decorator, or find a friend who actually likes decorating, since it's fairly clear that you don't!

  • Also, consider that when you look at YOUR task it can seem insurmountable but when you look at the same task confronting another person, it can seem simple. Two action plans flow from this single observations:

    1) Ask for help, as suggested. Since it is YOUR problem, other people will have no baggage and will love to help and will gain a sense of satisfaction from it. They will also feel motivated to tackle some of their own baggage laden dragons. (My, now that really is an analogy run amok. ;-) Part of this is perfectionism. You would agonize over doing just the perfect job. They would just paint the damn wall and be done with it ... it is not THEIRS after all. And you know, once it is done, you'll probably barely notice the slight "imperfections" in the job they did. More likely, you'll move on and be glad it is all over. Even if you did, you'd be too well-mannered to criticize.

    2) Role Playing: When I've been stuck and immobilized I've found success in imagining that I am helping someone else. This is not a new idea by any means, but a good one to keep in your pocket. (Damn, another nutty analogy ;-) I think Mark even uses this one in his GED book, IIRC ... Lakein certainly does. You just engage in a bit of MPD, put on your "hired help" hat, and come to work for yourself to fix the mess. The emotional distance this creates is often enough for you to see your way out of the box and get moving.

    "O.K. I've been hied to paint this room. No problem. This silly twit wants me to do the whole thing by myself. Screw that. I'm holding a painting party. YAY! Paint ... paint ... paint. Pass the beer." ;-)
