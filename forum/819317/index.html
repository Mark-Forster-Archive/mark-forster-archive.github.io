AF2: Closed Lists and Speed

  • After slightly more than 24 hours of using AF2, I consider myself one of the world's foremost experts on this revolutionary system. ;)

    1. I find it to be an extraordinarily nimble system. Example: I have "NY Times" as the current item. I am reading the NY Times. Then I think of something else. I write it at the end of my list. I decide it's important to do now. So, I re-add "NY Times" and do this important-now item. Then I go back to reading the Times. Then I help my wife deal with our mouse problem (after writing that on my list, followed by "NY Times" again). Then I go back to the Times. In AF1, this kind of quick reaction was usually not possible, unless, serendipitously, I was on the last page and there were a lot of blank lines left on the last page. So far, I have found this nimbleness to be a very positive attribute, rather than a distraction.

    Mark has written about being "on list" 24 hour-a-day. I don't have any near-term plans to try this myself, but it is very significant that one could even consider this. That is a sign of the sensitivity of AF2 compared to AF1. I never even would have toyed with the 24-hour idea with AF1.

    2. I will now put forward the idea that AF2 still incorporates the idea of a closed list. Admittedly, the definition of closed might need to be reinterpreted slightly, but I do not think it damages the core concept. I have designated as the "chopping block" the items that Mark calls "on notice for dismissal." My chopping block is my closed list. I can't add to that list. I can only do or dismiss the items on that list. That really focuses my attention. I find this aspect much more DIT-like than AF1-like and I like it. With DIT and the chopping block, I tend to have a smaller number of items. And I know that they are high priorities for today. With AF1, it was rarely a case that there were items that were marked "do or die" for any particular day.

    I think the definition of a closed list is a list to which we cannot add new items (DIT pages 28, 146).

    By this definition, each pass from the latest to the oldest is an open list, because we can add to it as we work it.

  • Moises,

    that was what I thought. But I have changed my mind.

    You cannot add a new task before your starting point. Admittedly, you only pick one item before the next pass, so you cannot reasonably hope to finish it, but your choice is limited to a closed list. Next pass you have a different closed list including anything you added while doing the first pass.

    With AF1, on one pass of the list you are working on an open list of closed lists (!).
