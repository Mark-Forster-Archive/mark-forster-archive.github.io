  • I think we have to make our decisions based on how strongly we feel about the task at hand and how strong our gut instinct is. To a degree that will depend upon the importance of the task and the penalty for not meeting the deadline - if we were due to appear in court then the "I didn't feel it was the right time" reason might not be appreciated and we can be pretty sure about the penalty.

    From experience so far, when a task gets to dismissal stage it is clear why it is being dismissed or alternatively what the reason for the resistance is so that the course of action can be chosen in an informed way.

    If you are reviewing your lists regularly a crtitical task is unlikely to get missed - if you decide not to action it it will be more likely to be a conscious and informed decision than otherwise. Your subconscious is after all aware of the deadlines involved.

    However at the end of the day you can only see what happens when it happens - hypothetical doesn't work well with AF because your subconscious knows it is only hypothetical.

    There's a saying that we need to decide which bridges to cross and which to burn - we cannot do either until we reach them.

  • I don't think you could ever dismiss a mission critical item (other than rephrasing it to make it more "doable" or transferring it to your calendar) because then if you could dismiss it, it wouldn't be mission critical. It may be the last thing on your list and it may not stand out in the way that items stand out when a page is new but some things are always going to be horrible to start. Why AF is so powerful is that it creates another option which is worse - dismissing the task which effectively adds another task to the page in addition to the one you are resisting and you can do either but you have to do one. If the task is important that in comparison that horrible task doesn't seem so horrible (much less horrible than dismissing it when it's mission critical).

    If you started finding yourself dismissing items that were truly mission critical then that would be a signal that you were not committed to the project and if the project was a fundamental part of your job, that you were not committed to your job. Could be your subconscious saying that you need a job change?
