The Cult of Done

  • I stumbled across this while following links around from a discussion here, so it seems appropriate to post it back. I don't know whether the following has been posted here before, but I liked it.

    THE CULT OF DONE MANIFESTO

    The Cult of Done Manifesto

    1. There are three states of being. Not knowing, action and completion.
    2. Accept that everything is a draft. It helps to get it done.
    3. There is no editing stage.
    4. Pretending you know what you're doing is almost the same as knowing what you are doing, so just accept that you know what you're doing even if you don't and do it.
    5. Banish procrastination. If you wait more than a week to get an idea done, abandon it.
    6. The point of being done is not to finish but to get other things done.
    7. Once you're done you can throw it away.
    8. Laugh at perfection. It's boring and keeps you from being done.
    9. People without dirty hands are wrong. Doing something makes you right.
    10. Failure counts as done. So do mistakes.
    11. Destruction is a variant of done.
    12. If you have an idea and publish it on the internet, that counts as a ghost of done.
    13. Done is the engine of more.

    The original posting, with some nifty posters in the comments, can be found at http://www.brepettis.com/blog/2009/3/3/the-cult-of-done-manifesto.html

  • No, I'm not joking, particularly. I'm a big believer in George Patton's maxim that it's better to have a good plan today than a perfect plan tomorrow. Perfection is unachievable, it's silly to try: it only becomes a higher-order form of procrastination. Learn when "good enough" is good enough.

    Similarly, "not knowing" can become a form of procrastination. You may not <I>know, </i>but you can guess, surmise and hypothesize, and then test those assumptions (which is to say, "pretend to know what you're doing") and see where you get. You might learn something.

    Obsession and overwork may occasionally produce greatness (or more likely exist alongside it in some instances), but they more often produce anxiety, stress, hypertension, heart disease, ulcers, frustration, family problems and an early demise. I don't need to be that great.

    I suspect folks need to think about what "done" means in a given context. Salk did not put an item on his "To Do" list which said "Invent polio vaccine", in order to be able to cross it off a dozen years later. This manifesto is tactical, not strategic.

    You said: "I suspect that you have been brought up doing battle with the opposite problem in America, so you naturally see this as dangerous and inappropriate."

    In a way, yes. I've had lots of problems (in business) with people running off "half-cocked" to start projects. My career was in software and system development. I can't tell you how costly it is when people start coding before they start thinking. The problem is all to prevalent and very costly. Just think about how much Windows drives you crazy. It is basically because they did not think through the implications of what they had in mind before they went ahead with it.

    That said, it IS possible to over think. It is possible to strive for unachievable perfection and lose the advantage of agility and speed. But I don't think that the answer is to make a "cult" of "good enough is good enough". There was a business philosophy some years ago (in the early '70's if memory serves) called "Zero Defects". It has fallen by the wayside for some reason ... probably because it was just a buzz word in the first place. But the example given to justify it was:

    "So you think 99.6% is good enough? What if your plane had a 99.6% chance of landing safely? Would you think that was good enough?"

    The point is that for some things, perfection must be the standard ... nothing else is acceptable.

    Last point ... as with so many other things (ZD, 6-Sigma, Vision and Mission Statements, MBO, etc.) this is often about buzz words to keep investors thinking that business has discovered some wonderful new philosophy which will give them an edge and so earn the investors more money. As P.T. Barnum pointed out: "There's a sucker born every minute ... and two to take him." So long as we don't see through the hype and buzz words and note that none of this is anything new and most of it is meaningless drivel ... that is how long we will be suckers. ;-) Yes, "Strike while the iron is hot." but also remember that "Fools rush in where angels fear to tread." For every platitude there is an opposite, and just as wise sounding, one. (I wonder if anyone has cataloged them all and arranged them in contradictory pairs? LOL ;-)
