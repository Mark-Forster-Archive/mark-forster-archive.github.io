    I agree with you. Furthermore, I think that Mark's Little and Often Principle preserves the tiny kernel of truth that lies within Allen's "next physical action" notion.

    Where Allen went wrong was in requiring me to WRITE down the next physical action. Where Mark went right was in requiring me to DO any physical action (as in the paradigmatic "I'll just open the project folder").

    My problem with Allen was that how I describe an action changes all the time. If I am charging forward and energized, I will say to myself "I am going to start writing this proposal." The point with L&O is that I can just write on my list "XYZ project" and as long as I do some physical action, I have (a) overcome inertia and started work on the project, and (b) I get the reward of being able to mark the item as done and the concomitant good feeling of knowing that I have moved the project forward.

  • Norman -
    <<<Why not see if they stand out and let them go if they don't? For some reason people are afraid of letting go of tasks they just are not doing. The salient question is why? >>>

    You may disagree, but I don't think it's just a neurotic resistance to letting things go. There's more to it than that, at least for me.

    For some tasks, it's not a problem letting them go. They were usually hare-brained ideas in the first place, or I am just not committed to them. These aren't the ones facing expiration, at least not for me. After seeing these kinds of tasks on my list repeatedly for several days, I realize I don't need to do them, don't want to do them, am not going to do them, and just delete them.

    A few of them really do stay around and end up "expiring", but by far most of the tasks facing expiration are not that kind of task. Rather, they are tasks associated with some kind of commitment, and if I just let the task expire, then I am reneging on that commitment. This is not acceptable to me. If I am going to back out of a commitment, then I need to do that explicitly, and also work it out with any other people that may be involved. It can affect my wife, my family, my coworkers, my boss, etc.

    Also, tasks of this kind don't have any problem "standing out" for me. The problem is just that there are too many of them, and not enough hours in the day.

    More than any of the AF variations, DWM is helping me identify this kind of thing. It is helping me realize what is getting in the way of meeting some of those commitments; to see what will really be required to meet them; and forcing me to decide what to drop, or at least what to re-think.

    The AF1 dismissal process didn't really help with this -- my AF list just kept growing and growing. As time went on, the number of open tasks never really went down. I did lots of work, got lots of things done, but couldn't keep up with all the new things I needed to do. AF4 helped with that a little, but every time I started a new "closed list" it was longer than the previous one -- and never shorter than 5 or 6 pages.

    The DWM "space invaders" marching forward every day really helps me see how much is on my plate and how much I have over-committed -- in this way, DWM is very much like DIT but much more flexible. Also, seeing what kinds of things are expiring and thinking about why, is giving me lots of ideas for improving my systems for handling my work, not just re-thinking my commitments.

    Anyway, that's how it's working for me, and it's great!
