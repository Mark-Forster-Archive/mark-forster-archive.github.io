  • Eugene:
    This is something fundamental. Older task management systems aim at what you are looking for: to be in control. AF aims at something else: to be in the flow of things. Things almost "happening by themselves". Almost effortless. And this is because it works in harmony with your subconscious (or however you want to call it), instead of fueling a constant fight of rational thinking against the rest of you.

    Do I recommend to go back to GTD? No. Because, if you think about it, the feeling to be in control is an illusion. You're in control until something unexpected happens - and this is always the case. Such is life. Or, as any bankrobber knows: Plans go wrong - always ;-)

    With AF, you can let go your firm grip on life - and that's a relief. It needs, however, to trust the system (haven't we heard that a lot a year ago?), and boy, THAT'S difficult when you're SO used to control everything! I have "managed" every single one of my days since 1984 - and I was SO tired of it... without knowing it. It was that tiredness that made me think "hey, wow", when I came across AF.

    Today, when I look at my dismissed lists, I see a lot of tasks that I haven't done - and good so, because they were unnecessary tasks right from the beginning. Only that I wasn't unable to realize it at the time I jotted them down. It was my subconscious that figured it out, along with the AF process.

    So. A weekly review? How about simply reading through your list from time to time? That's simple and does the job.

  • Eugene,

    I don't think I understand you. And I think you didn't understand what I wrote either. :-/

    I did not mean your list should only have 6 most important projects. I meant your list should still have 100 actions. Next, entirely other than that list, you should post your 6 [or more or less] most important projects where you will see them.

    Keep the 6 as an ever-present reminder of what is important.

    Now work through your list of 100 as you feel is right. Pick tasks related to your 6 as often as possible. When that's not possible, pick other tasks. And if you feel like you are going too long not doing important stuff, stop to figure what's stopping you and remedy that:

    - add a new project-related task on the list
    - clarify something on your list
    - take action to better prepare a stuck task

    In summary, work your AF list, but focus on the important tasks it contains.
