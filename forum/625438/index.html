A practical example of changed focus .....

  • With AF working on an intuitive level as opposed to a rational level, it can often be difficult to get our thoughts around the concept of that working in practice. I experienced a very clear example of how that worked today which I thought I would share in case it helped explain the concept to anyone else ........

    Today I misplaced an item. It was nothing important but pre AF I would have immediately spent time looking for it, beating myself up in the process about having lost it in the first place, and once I had found it would have allowed the distraction to lead me on to other means of procrastination.

    Today I thought, well it's around somewhere and will turn up in due course. I added it my list as "find x" I was interested to note as I wrote it that I wrote "find x" and not "look for x" "Look for x" would have created a distraction and I instictively knew that it would turn up without me having to get in a flap looking for it. The task "find x" was more of a memory aid (along the lines of "has Bob returned my call?") than a task (probably should have been "found x?"). Half an hour later I found a different item that I had not put away, and therefore was "misplaced". As I picked it up to put away I was not only reminded that I had a misplaced item on my list but recalled instantly where I had put it. The funny think was that I had moved it from it's normal place because I thought I needed to find a new place for it BUT I hadn't used AF to task that.

    This was a really trivial item (and I wouldn't have considered any need to put it's relocation on a list) BUT it's loss would previously have become a major procrastinator for me. What was a really interesting discovery for me was the impact of AF, not in that I almost "magically" recalled where the item was but in the fact that, putting it on the list, AF automatically released my mind from thinking about it and enabled that recall to happen.
