Postponing a Column 2 Task?

  • On the topic of proper handling of Column 2, here's a situation I am dealing with right now.

    A couple weeks ago, my manager asked me to take on a small side project, to do some spreadsheet work for someone in another part of the organization. I always like doing spreadsheet work so I gladly agreed to take a look.

    After meeting with the person who needed the spreadsheet, and agreeing to do a first draft within the next week or two, I entered the task in Column 1: "Spreadsheet 1st draft".

    The task soon "stood out", and I started working on it. It then got re-entered in Column 2.

    A few days later, my main work went into high gear -- running a pilot on some software that our group will be using. I'm coordinating our team's pilot work, and this task has quickly taken all my attention.

    For this main project, SF is working beautifully.

    However, there's that nasty spreadsheet clogging up Column 2. It's always the last thing left in Column 2. I really don't have time for it right now. In retrospect, I never should have taken on the project, knowing that our pilot work would be starting soon. But that's in retrospect. Right now, I've still got this rock sitting in Column 2 that I need to deal with.

    I already emailed the guy who needs the spreadsheet -- he's fine if it takes a week or two longer before I deliver it.

    But the project is not done. It just needs to be postponed for a week or two.

    Now it seems the logical thing to do would be to say "done for now" and put it into an Outlook reminder for a week or so from today, and then I'd reenter into Column 1 at that point.

    But I really want to follow the SF rules as strictly as possible, both in letter and in spirit. But this puts me in a difficulty. The project is NOT done. I AM still committed to do the project -- I CANNOT just delete it. But I CAN'T really give it any attention right now.

    So what I have been doing, whenever that project is keeping me from moving to the next page, is setting the timer for 5 minutes and opening the file and reading over the associated emails, just to keep the ideas in my mind to let my subconscious percolate on it.

    But I feel like my subconscious already has plenty of other work to do. :-) This 5 minutes seems like annoying busywork -- but that seems to be what the rules are telling me to do.

    Mark, any advice on the best way to handle this, in full keeping with the intent of the SF rules?

    Thanks!!


    (Ah, finally, I can move that task forward, now that I've written this post about it!!) :-)

    I think GTD is giving us misleading assumptions about what "time management system" can mean to Mark Forster (and I suspect this annoys him a little bit).
    It's pretty obvious that David Allen and him have a very different way of providing us "systems" to adress our time management issues. They focus on very different areas of the broad "time management" scope.

    They both cover the whole spectrum, but David Allen's "system" mainly goes into much details about the "organization" part of TM :
    GTD takes you by the hand and teaches you, step by step, a very precise, systematic and defined way of breaking up, sorting and organizing ALL of your data, projects and tasks, leaving you with a bunch of lists, along with some "best practices" advices (weekly review), some fundamental principles ("next action" model of inputting tasks) and a reminder system (tickler and calendar), but no real, efficient help on how to choose what to do and when.
    He gives us a lot of guidance and little flexibility in the "organization" part (which he calls "collecting" & "processing") / a lot of (too much?) flexibility with no real guidance in the "choosing what to do and when" part (which I would rather call "processing") - except the awkward concept of context/available energy/available time.

    MF with AF/DWM/SF pretty much takes the inverted approach to TM : his central "system" focuses on very cleverly giving us both guidance and (a sometimes too limited?) flexibility in the "choosing what to do and when" part to really help us actually Get Things Done. Everything else regarding TM surrounding the central system, including project management, data organization and management, and the reminders systems, is pretty much left up to us.
    He does provide us with a lot of great tips, suggestions and tools covering almost all of these areas, but he gives us, purposively I think, much more freedom and flexibility in the "organization" part vs GTD.
    We are free to choose whatever will work optimally with our personality, environment, and the varying natures of our projects. Mark actively encourages us to adopt any combination of strategies that may help us get our work done WITHOUT ever getting in our way.
    This seems to me like the much better approach vs David Allen's one.

    If the GTD system of organizing things happens to work well for someone under a certain set of circumstances, I don't see how or why it coudlnt be compatible with the SF system of tasks processing ... Just get rid of contexts (or keep only 2 : work and personal, with a SF notebook for each) and dont give as much importance to the "next action" way of inputting tasks, and you are left with a valid set of options for all the TM-related "stuff" that surrounds the central SF system. It might not be what Mark would choose for himself, but it might work for some people.
