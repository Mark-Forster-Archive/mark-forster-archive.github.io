Accumulating Resistance

  • I have a theory that no optimal AF system will be found. Why:

    Actual work is complex and varied. A system that tries to accommodate that will always have a certain emphasis and a certain deemphasis. This bias may be small, but it makes the stuff it favours easier and other stuff harder. Over time this bias will accumulate as a memory embedded in the pattern of tasks done and not done. You will start to notice the tasks which don't as easily get tackled within the system, and resistance to these tasks accumulate. The system will start to bother you just a bit.

    The answer seems to be simple: A good AF system has a slight bias against certain tasks. As that bias becomes perceptible, bothersome, shift the rules a bit to bias in favor of those tasks.The problem melts away, as those tasks get cleared and a new bias becomes evident.

    If I'm right, then the ultimate autofocus is the one in which the rules change (very slightly) from month to month according to the situation.

    Having said that, I believe SFv3 is better than everything that preceded it because of its smooth blending of urgent and unfinished tasks. But even SF isn't optimal. The problems are smaller, and regular small adjustments will keep it effective.

  • That is sound theory, Alan. You make a good case.

    But don't forget about all the adjustments we can make without even touching the rules, by tinkering with project management and phrasing.

    For example, maybe I've been resisting "read War & Peace," using Mark's approach of keeping it interminably in C2, and I hypothesize that it's due to not breaking the project into small tasks. Then I experiment with "read W&P for an hour," but that doesn't fly either, so I try "read W&P 20 pages." Eventually, I discover that the optimal phrase is, "read W&P one paragraph," which typically results in frequent reading sessions of 45 minutes each.

    Or, suppose I am in the (accidental) habit of writing one action per project on my list. And suppose several of my projects are stuck because each of their single actions is highly resistable. Through dismissal, I catch on to this, so I begin writing as many options as possible for stuck projects, including mutually exclusive options such as "buy red notebook" and "buy blue notebook," or "call Bob with bad news" and "email Bob with bad news."

    More freedom exists in SF's lack of a requirement to finish tasks before crossing them off. I tend to forget, especially in C2, that I can move to the next page just by "actioning" those items, rather than completing them all the way. Endless little games can be made out of this. We can say that when a task has been in C2 for longer than we'd like, we will start working it in strictly limited ten minute blocks, etc. When the timer rings, I *must* stop and cross it out ... then sit back and watch the power of reverse psychology!

    It would take me a long time to play with all those combinations before I got around to the rules themselves.

    But broadly speaking, yes, these sorts of adjustments are an essential part of running SuperFocus.

  • I don't think the tasks are what create the resistance -- and I don't think it's the way theyare written, either. I think it's the way they are *framed*. And the *rules* determine how they are framed.

    In Mark Forster's case, he's finding that the obligation to clear C2 before moving to the next page is creating resistance to those C2 tasks, and ultimately to the whole system. Why even pick up the SF notebook, when you KNOW there are those nasty tasks waiting there that you MUST clear before you can really do anything else? Hmm, maybe I'll go read a book or go for a walk or surf the Internet for a few minutes first, and THEN open my book. Oops.

    In my case, with AF1, I was always chasing the end of the list, never cycling back to the beginning, and thus never getting repeated exposure to the tasks, and never triggering dismissal. I became numb to the whole list as a result, and was always in a state of anxiety. I relied on Outlook reminders as a backup to remind me of deadlines. It just became unworkable.

    With DWM, I almost always cycled through the first 7 days (from TODAY through TODAY+7) and never got past that. So, it was essentially the same problem as AF1 -- too many things, not seeing them often enough, not cycling fast enough. DWM got the job done more reliably, because I could always make something "urgent" by entering it at TODAY+7. But it lost the intuitive feel and "flow" of AF. It still "worked" more effectively than other systems I'd tried -- but it became dull and monotonous, and I often became numb to it overall.

    In all three of these examples, the system is what ultimately creates the resistance -- not the tasks. And I'm not convinced it's impossible to address those various problems by introducing new or modified rules.

    The reason I think it's possible is because Mark has already achieved success several times.

    For example, with AF1, there was no effective mechanism to handle urgency. Thus a "common sense override" had to be invoked frequently. This led to lack of trust in the system, and ultimately, to resistance to using the system overall (at least, for those who had lots of urgent things to deal with). One could get by only with various external supports such as Outlook reminders or "today" lists or whatever. Mark fixed that by introducing Column 2 for urgent tasks. This fix alone went very far in achieving a balance between "urgent" and "important" that AF1 was not able to achieve.

    That's just one example, of course, and there are many others. With each turn of the TM crank, Mark keeps finding new innovative ways to address these systemic issues.
    Personally, I don't have a resistance problem with C2, at least not yet. If I do feel resistance, I remind myself that I just need to take action, not actually finish anything. That's enough to help me push through a long C2 list. Also, I always reserve the right to declare a task "done for now" -- I usually find that there's a reason why I'm resisting one of these C2 tasks, and the reason is that the raison d'etre of that task has changed in some significant way, and when that happens, it's "done for now" -- it's really turned into something new.

    Maybe I'll develop more resistance later, or maybe find some other aspect of the system that starts pushing back at me. But I would expect these trouble spots to be issues with the SYSTEM, not issues with the way I am writing tasks. As Bernie wrote, these systems already encourage you to experiment with different ways of writing tasks to find whatever works for you. The more difficult problems arise when, no matter how you write your tasks, the system starts to get stuck somewhere and you can't make progress, you can't get into the flow.

  • Essentially, I am agreeing with all this.

    My one reservation is that some of us need *not* be told to tinker with the rules, or we will gladly tinker away the hours in the name of productivity. I would rather leave the rules as a very, very last resort. After all, with sufficiently extensive rule changes, I could end up with some two-column version of GTD that includes a "Tomorrow" context.

    In a really good system, the rules are so generally enabling that you forget you are following them. David Allen (ironically) gives the example of lines on the road. We may jockey around with options for passing, what speed to go,when to turn, but we take the lines for granted. They are so valuable in creating freedom of movement (by eliminating oncoming traffic from our lane) that we wouldn't want to mess with them.

    SuperFocus always seemed like that sort of system to me, so this is why I am loath to endorse ongoing rule changes. For example, I have noticed that Mark's newest "parking" variation is essentially what I had created by framing my projects a certain way. It is more direct and natural using his rule change, but the neat thing is that I was able to get there without "breaking the rules." I do think we should experiment to discover good rules (as Mark continues to do), but it will really help if the rules end up fixed once discovered.

    I would liken all this to the cross-training or plateauing that occurs in fitness and exercise. Even on a healthy, effective routine, there will be blind spots, and your body will eventually adapt to slow further change. Many athletes rotate amongst two or three favorite regimens, but they avoid the distraction of endlessly experimenting on themselves.

    Perhaps, rather than "changing the rules," we can rotate occasionally amongst our three favorite variations? For some, there is no distinction here, but for others (ahem) this is rather crucial.
