  • Mark,

    That about sums it up. I honestly don't think that Objectivism is all that bad as a philosophy. IOW, it tends to break down when the people who read the books try to implement it. There is nothing inherent in the philosophy that argues against being kind or charitable, for example, but those who I hung with when I was in the movement were harsh and even brutal. And really proud of it!

    It is a question of personality I believe. I remember at one Objectivist "party" (now does that idea short circuit something in your brain? ;-) I was talking to a young lady. She was waxing on about the wonders of Rachmaninoff (one of my favorite composers, as it turns out) and I said something about Gershwin or someone else being a favorite of mine as well. GAK! I got a stare that would stop a moving train. Rachmaninoff was the "sanctioned" Objectivist composer and how could I DARE to like the music of someone else? that look told me.

    As it happened Branden drifted by just then and this young lady tagged him and posed the question "in that voice": "What do you think of the music of George Gershwin?" Oh boy, did she ever plan for me to get my skin scalded off. When he said: "Oh, I love his Concerto in F." (or something like that ... it's been so many years and so many parties ago ;-) she looked as if her world came to an end. She asked how he could like Gershwin when Rachmaninoff was so much "better" and he just shrugged and said something along the lines of "his music makes me smile" or something. I probably did not see it then, but I'm sure that young lady was already beginning to look for a better movement where her heroes were more absolutist. Thinking back, I believe that before she drifted to Objectivism she was a member of the John Birch Society. Figures.

    As to the novel, yes, there is a bit of science fiction there. I don't mind that in general, but when you are trying to PROVE something with your novel, then having the outcome hinge on something like that is a bit of a "deus ex machina". I did love Atlas as a novel, though. I remember the day I read it ... actually it was the night. I finished the damn thing, cover to cover, in a night. GAK! But I did skip over that speech, or rather chapter long tirade ;-)

    I think that there are some very important ideas in the philosophy of Objectivism. I think that those who are attracted to it as a movement, and who don't have a fully balanced view of the world make it a late night TV joke. Rand herself was not the best face to put on it. She had an awful childhood and it warped her into something of a malevolent person. Her draconian moralism was born of her own pain and she did not see the world as a happy place. For her, life was noble struggle against the forces of evil. She would deny it, but that was my observation. Anyone who was an Objectivist then said: "Yes but ..." was excommunicated. For her, it had to be all or nothing. Either you were for her or against her. I doubt she really cared much which.

    I can hear you say that any philosophy that brings out the worst in people can't be much good. I've struggled with that idea and have not resolved it yet. Certainly there are ideas that sound good in theory but fail in practice. Socialism is one ... Capitalism is another. Both require that human nature be a certain way in order for them to work in practice. But human nature is not as required for either of those systems to work, IMO.

    Another feature of Objectivism that causes problems is that it sees human beings as automatons. Either you are good or bad ... you can't be simply confused, or conflicted, or in doubt or ... That is why Branden was doomed to be an outcast. The very idea of an "Objectivist Psychologist" is something of a contradiction in terms. You can't be in the helping professions and not see people as ... well, human.

    I have to say that my experience with Objectivism was educational. It did not drive me to Catholicism ;-) but it did help me understand how living life according to a theory and being shut off from observation of what was "real", leads to error. Thinking back now, I don't know a single "close friend" I had from those years. One way or another things always ended in some kind of draconian judgment and "poof" they were gone.

  • Wow Mark, who knew you had been into Objectivism too?!? (see, that's why I like this forum, nobody I know in my 'real life' would know what I was talking about - except my son.)

    Barbara Branden posted the following quote from N.B. on Objectivist Living (trying to help a fellow Objectivist determine the right goals for them):

    "When admirers of Ayn Rand seek my services professionally, they often come with the secret hope, rarely acknowledged in words, that with Nathaniel Branden they will at last become the masters of repression needed to fulfill the dream of becoming an ideal objectivist. When I tell them, usually fairly early in our relationship, that one of their chief problems is that they are out of touch with their feelings and emotions, cut off from them and oblivious, and that they need to learn how to listen more to their inner signals, to listen to their emotions, they often exhibit a glazed shock and disorientation. I guess I should admit that seeing their reaction is a real pleasure to me, one of the special treats of my profession you might say, and I do hope you will understand that I am acknowledging this with complete affection and good will and without any intention of sarcasm. The truth is, seeing their confusion and dismay, that it's hard to keep from smiling a little.

    "One of the first things I need to convey to them is that when they deny and disown their feelings and emotions, they really subvert and sabotage their ability to think clearly -- because they cut off access to too much vital information. This is one of my central themes in The Disowned Self. No one can be integrated, no one can function harmoniously, no one can think clearly and effectively about the deep issues of life who is oblivious to the internal signals, manifested as feelings and emotions, rising from within the organism. My formula for this is: "Feel deeply to think clearly." It seems, however, to take a long time -- for objectivists and nonobjectivists alike -- to understand that fully. Most of us have been encouraged to deny and repress who we are, to disown our feelings, to disown important aspects of the self, almost from the day we were born. The road back to selfhood usually entails a good deal of struggle and courage.

    "I know a lot of men and women who, in the name of idealism, in the name of lofty beliefs, crucify their bodies, crucify their feelings, and crucify their emotional life, in order to live up to that which they call their values. Just like the followers of one religion or another who, absorbed in some particular vision of what they think human beings can be or should be, leave the human beings they actually are in a very bad place: a place of neglect and even damnation. However, and this is a theme I shall return to later, no one ever grew or evolved by disowning and damning what he or she is. We can begin to grow only after we have accepted who we are and what we are and where we are right now. And no one was ever motivated to rise to glory by the pronouncement that he or she is rotten."
