Fun Things On the List

  • There seems to be a growing consensus that an AF list should include fun things.

    I have been resisting this and let me see if I can account for my reluctance:

    A key value of an AF list is that it facilitates action on a lot of things that I might find hard to do or hard to remember or hard to find time for. But fun things are not hard to do or hard to remember (though, admittedly, they might be neglected). Furthermore, lots of fun things are so vital to my sense of wellbeing that I give them a really high priority and schedule them as "virtually" time specific items (e.g. go for a swim at 4pm). (Similarly I would never put "eat lunch" on my list as there is not the remotest chance that I would forget lunch. So, again, I treat it as a time specific item even though it is not strictly speaking time specific.)

    Next, my AF list is for "work" (including lots of home jobs). But I sense that if I mix in "purely recreational" items amongst my tasks, they might "stand out" all too readily and I might be tempted to do them in preference to pressing items that I really want to do even though they might be difficult. (One danger with AF is that I might be tempted to do items in the order of easiest first and hardest last. And some would argue that, all else being equal, it might be better to tackle the hardest first.)

    Mind you, lots of my work is "fun" to me, and to that extent I am quite happy to include them on the list. But what I refer to in this post is things I would never regard as "work". (e.g. going to the pictures, watching TV, going for a stroll around the block)

    Finally, there is one aspect of Mark's "Instructions" that I do not recall anyone raising. It is this: "This system can be quite addictive (at least that is what I've found) so you may find it is a good idea to set strict working hours. Stop dead when the time is up..." Clearly, when these instructions were written, Mark visuallized AF as applying only to "work". That's where I think I still stand and I am inclined to regard the introduction of purely fun things as detrimental to the system, if that's not too strong a way of putting it.

  • Hi Jim M,

    I can see some of the logic you're using, but as aone-man business working almost exclusively away from home, I have separated my AF lists into Office/Work (which includes my home office or the assignment/client's office) and Personal.

    The reason is that there's a limit of Personal stuff I can actually do on assignment (especially if there's flaky Internet connection like here!), and when at home/home office, it helps me keep the focus on the one or the other, otherwise I flit from one task to the other without any direction or control.

    But then some people, Christine B, like to pile it all onto one list, it seems to work for her. That's the thing which some of us forget: AF isn't a formula anything like GTD etc, it's up to each of us to adopt or adapt the AF Principles Mark F has provided.

    Mind you, I do sometimes mix in a Personal item in the Office/Work list, so i don't forget. For example, I entered "Book TV in lodge lounge to watch highlights of South Africa's ODI cricket victory" followed by "Check see when DVD of SA Tour to Australia 2008/9 will be available."

    I suppose it's sometimes how you define "fun", and i take your point about deriving fun from work tasks and vice versa, thank goodness.

  • Hi Bev

    In answer to your question I have actually tried both, and find that using as much of my discretionary time as possible on my lists works best. However that is because I am putting a lot of routine and fun things on my list as well.

    I also have a lot less resistance to doing items if I am doing them from the list (because I am actioning them when they stand out) rather than outside the list. This past week has been particularly interesting because I was having to work with some larger than usual projects on a more intensive basis than I suspect I would have had to do had I been using AF for longer. Consequently things like checking email were being done "off list" but I had quite a resistance, even though I was dealing with them. It was interesting to note how today, when I went back to my lists properly, I cleared many more emails and more easily, than when actioning that same task outside of AF.

    There is clear evidence for me that working with my lists dramatically improves my productivity and when I am not working from them I can feel that sense of "I've got so much to do" starting to return. By using them, or at the very least reviewing them, within my discretionary time, I can actually choose a task like "catch up on recorded TV shows" (which became a task as my favourite procrastinator was being ignored) and catch up on TV without guilt that I should be doing something else. Generally that would be in the evening but I have also found that I can catch up on some shows whilst filing, so do two tasks at once! Wouldn't recommend it in an office tho'!
