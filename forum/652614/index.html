Restricting the number of items actioned per page?

  • Has anyone experimented with restricting the number of items they do on each visit to a page or with having a set number that they do?

    I'm wondering what would happen if there was a rule that on each visit one did exactly half the items remaining on the page. This would work out very nicely if there were 32 items on the page to start with.To do this, there would probably also have to be a rule that you couldn't do any items on a page that was still open.

    I'm a bit reluctant to experiment with this myself, in case I mess up my perfectly good list - hence my request whether anyone has tried something similar.

  • Hi Mark

    I have used a "clear 50%" rule on tasks for years and it is a method that works but with high resistance. Consequently I rarely stick to it for long. The most successful system I used pre AF was a lot more structured and akin to David's 3x3 rule, and was the approach I used for paper and email and, but less successfully, for tasks. It had a lot of similarities to AF but was closer to the method you describe above.

    I would list the outstanding items in batches of 25. The rule was that I cleared 5 a day. They were closed lists so worked on the strucured procrastination basis but as you can probably imagine Friday was not a good day! In practice I usually cleared about 15 of the 25 items per list per week. At the end of the week I rolled everything up and relisted in batches of 25 again.

    I have to say that the system did work and worked pretty well for me. I cleared a considerable amount of backlog items and it was the one of the most effective systems I used for (a) keeping track and (b) making structured progress. The resistance was not as great as with other systems BUT there was still a significant amount of resistance which meant that eventually I had to just grit my teeth and do it.

    The reason that I would not wish to incorporate that type of system into AF is that, having done AF I can see exactly what the problems of that system were for me. The structure DID keep me going BUT it was not by any means fun! AF keeps me going AND is fun. Not only that, I have not had to "grit my teeth and just do it" with AF. When using the system (and obviously there are times at this stage in the game when I have had to come out of the system) I have had no procrastination and no resistance to the tasks I have actioned. When I have experienced resistance within the system it has caused me to question the reason - and so far, I think without exception, has identified the reason for that resistance. Consequently, through AF I have been able to address those reasons and in many cases remove the factors that caused it, which is the very reason why the tasks that I have actioned HAVE been able to be done without resistance.

    My old system worked in that it got things done. AF does more than that. It gets things done, yes, but so much more easily, and has also resulted in a myriad of other changes, to my systems, to my approach and to me! Significantly, what it has identified is that I do not like being told what to do, how to do it or when to do it - I'm obviously just a rebel at heart :-) Structure is important, and there is no doubt from reading this forum that many, many people do want that structure and thrive on it. I am just not one of them, unfortunately (I may have found things a lot easier if I were!)

    Mark, it is for that reason that I am not willing to tamper with AF in that way. For me it would impose restriction and detract from the free flowing nature of AF, putting me back into "must do" mode and blocking the need to look at reasons for resistance. I am not dismissing the idea out of hand per se, but want to continue "trusting the system" to tell me when to move on from a page.

    Hope that expands a little more on the reasoning behind my comment above :-)

  • Hi Mark, for about the first week of AF I wasn't sure what "moving round the page" meant. I thought once you hit the bottom of the page you moved on to the next page, rather than waiting for a complete pass through a page without taking any actions. This actually sped up the process of moving through the pages, because on many pages I would do just one item. However, since the first week of AF I have stuck with the original instructions: only move to the next page after a complete pass of the page without anything standing out. It slows things down a bit but more thoroughly deals with each page. Also, reading the same page several times imprints a lot of things in your brain (eg "pick up lightbulbs" -- might not stand out right now but when I'm out on the town I seem to remember those little tasks a lot better after reading them in my lists multiple times). Still, I keep thinking of going back my original approach simply because it's faster -- one initial read, then a second slower read to action things that jump out, but once you hit the bottom of the page, move to the next (ie don't go back up to the top again). However, I did tend to do things at the bottom of each page even if they didn't stand out, just to keep the list active and to avoid dismissing. Also, on the second scan through the list, I often find when I'm near the bottom of the page that something at the top is beginning to stand out, so I like the comfort of being able to go back up to the top again for a third pass through the items.

    To make a long story short, if there were to be a rule of X items per page, I'd tend to prefer fewer (eg one item) items per page rather than more. However, I think I prefer that there be no rule of "X" items.
