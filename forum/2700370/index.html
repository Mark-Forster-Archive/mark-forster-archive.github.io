Long lists

  • I've noticed a significant difference between the no-lists and the catch-all systems.

    In the most distilled no-list systems, items appear on the list by being done, and therefore we have a list of things we are currently working on.

    In a catch-all system, the list is really two sets of items - "tasks" which we are actually working on, and "ideas" for things we will do in the future. For example, The Simplest Time Management Method makes the distinction clear. Above the line, we find tasks, below the line we find ideas. Simple Scanning and many of the other long list systems intersperse these two categories, and Mark has offered various practices to help us determine a) how to move an idea into the task category, and b) how to determine which ideas are not ready to become tasks.

    I wonder what folks think about the importance of drawing this distinction. I'm currently using Simple Scanning, and feel like sometimes I would be better served by having separate lists for "I'm actively pursuing this and know it fits into my schedule" and "These would be nice to get around to once I have the resources."

  • My system of a few years back had a distinctive way of handling this:

    Take a notebook and build up a long list with the rule that new tasks are entered only on the left pages, and tasks you're working on are entered only on the right pages. When one of these pages is filled, close off both pages and start the next two pages.

    As with my current system (see Doting on My List), I would simple-scan the entire list, but circle about the last page (right side only). The effect was what you talked about: the left pages are ideas, and the right pages are active tasks.

    That said, I find the my current process more satisfying without this division. It has the switch to alternate between simple scanning and working active tasks (and new tasks).

  • Voluntas:

    << Is it fair to say that the first example means "attempt to" and the other means "work toward"? >>

    No.

    What Yoda is saying is that one should either set out to do something or decide not to do something. To say "I'll try to do it" is just a weasel way of sitting on the fence. A few examples: "I'll try to get to your party on Saturday" (meaning I probably won't but don't want to say so), "I'll try to give up smoking starting next week" (meaning I probably won't succeed in giving it up for more than a few days, or quite likely a few hours).

    Your reaction to someone saying something like that is that they're not very likely to do what they say.

    On the other hand ""I will try to fit you in for your next session this weekend" means exactly what it says. I'l juggle my other appointments and see if there are some gaps and if it's at all possible I'll fit you in.

    Your reaction to someone saying this is that there is a high probability that they will succeed in fitting you in.

    When Khatzumoto says: "Do try to increase the probability that they will get done". He means it in the second sense. He is encouraging you to think about and experiment with ways to increase the probability. You may not succeed but there is a high probability that you will.
