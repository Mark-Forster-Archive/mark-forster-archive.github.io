  • Bev:

    There are two ways of doing a task like this:

    1) Do one lesson plan at a time.

    or

    2) Divide the making of a lesson plan up into steps, then do Step 1 for each of the 14 plans, then Step 2, and so on.

    Method 2 is more mechanical and as such requires less investment of energy, so you may find it provokes less resistance in you.

  • Far and away the best tool I've come across for thinking out projects like these is a mindmap program. I use Mindjet mindmanager and have been able to pull off some bigger projects in large part because of this brilliant mindmapping program. You get to organize ideas as linear or nonlinear as you like. And it is much faster than trying to work with one sheet of paper at a time or going at it from a to z. With mindmanager you can even do a lot of basic word processing inside the program, and work with all kinds of information sources, links, images, tables, etc.

    I turned my partner to it (she's a teacher) and loves using it to organize her lessons. If you need to organize information it's in my opinion the best way to go about it.

    I think working sequentially is great if you have trouble starting but in a lot of cases the more brain friendly and efficient way is by starting where your mind feels like going first, or what seems like the best place to start. (for example starting a thesis research paper at the very beginning of the text is often a bad place to start because it's function is to lead in to the rest of the paper (which is hard to do properly if the rest of the text is not written yet!)).

    If you use a tool like mindmapping, you can stay organized working this way.

  • Bev:

    << However, your overall point is that each part of a project is a step, no matter what the method used, correct? >>

    My overall point was that it's quicker to do each step for all projects together, than to complete one project then start on the next and so on.

    In this particular example, you can:

    EITHER
    mindmap, then summarise, then find supplemental material, then organise points for Topic One. Then repeat the process for Topic Two and so on.

    OR
    mindmap all topics, then summarise all topics, then find supplemental material for all topics, then organise points for all topics.

    The second method is generally faster more efficient and generates less resistance.

    In Autofocus I would simply put in a task "Mindmap Topics" and keep re-entering it until it was completed. Then I would start a new task "Summarise Topics" and so on.

  • Bev:

    The real question is whether you are reasonably satisfied with the way you are operating now. If you are then there is no point changing things.

    If on the other hand you are not satisfied, then it is worthwhile learning a new way of doing things. In that case I would suggest the following:

    1) Don't have the book sitting by the side of your desk - you should keep it open on your desk.

    2) Don't allow yourself to do *anything* workwise other than what the system shows you. Force yourself to use the system for everything. You'll soon get into the habit of it.

    3) Don't fill the list with everything you can think of straight away. That's a pretty good way of sinking any system. Start by putting the things of immediate concern on the list, and then add further tasks as you think of them or as they come up.
