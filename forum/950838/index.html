    About getting children to do what you want, I'm a complete ignoramus. But I thought this was interesting: In response to the question "What do you want to do when you grow up", a child (who seems to like arts and music) said "Whatever they want me to do", and the question to the group was, how do you encourage this child to pursue his interests when he doesn't want to work at it.

    Here's my ignorant thinking: You can decide the child's response (I'll do what I'm told) is good, and say "Okay I want you to practice music". Or you could decide it's bad and get the child to grow into expressing and pursuing his/her own interests.

    Personally I think a combination of both is best. Listening to instruction, and taking initiative are both valuable skills. How you do that, I don't know. I think Covey's approach of delegating responsibility [get your child to accept responsibility for the quality of his/her work] has merit.

  • Well I object to closing the thread since Mike got the last word! And more than one post with the last word too. :-)

    Mel, I don't know about this:

    "if they're committed to a sport or lesson and then decide they don't like it, they have to finish out the season."

    Although that's always been my philosophy as well for both my kids (and myself), I'm starting to question if this is a good thing. (It's also kind of back to the process vs. outcome thinking - do you value that kid loving the process or getting the certificate/putting in the time?)

    Following this for work has at times kept me in a job when I could see in the first 2 weeks that it wasn't the right environment. But I would tell myself to finish out the year, then better make it two because you don't want to look like you're hopping around on your resume. Although it has been a pattern that 18 months has been as much as I could stand before giving myself permission to quit. That's 17 1/2 months of my life I will never get back.

    But more than that, by continuing to force yourself into a situation where you are not naturally good at the sport or the job and don't like it means that you will not perform well (yet you try to force yourself), which is like dying a little inside. Then your self confidence goes, and so on...

    Oh, I've made the same mistake in relationships too, not questioning my gut that something wasn't right and it was ok to be a quitter.

    With just the sports / arts thing: After having an extremely independent little guy, I realized that more progress was made and less frustration for everyone involved if we focused on individual instruction where possible for non-team activities. Classes in general are taught at the median level so if someone is exceptionally talented or not at all, they lose out. Particularly if they are exceptionally good as the less talented ones usually get more help. I've had a session of skiing lessons regress him 2 years in ability and create fears that were not there to begin with. What a waste.

    Also making them stick with soccer (which pretend they doesn't like - and both of mine didn't) means that they don't have the time/opportunity to participate in baseball or karate or whatever. And kids will rebel and show their displeasure in having to do something they don't want in the most maddening of ways. For both my kids, they would drag their heels, not put any energy into it and basically make it obvious that they would not participate no way, no how. There in body (barely), but not in mind. (They get this from me.) LOL

    I did the same thing with piano lessons as a child. Both sisters took years of lessons and one had a minor in music in University. I made my mother let me quit after 4 or 5 years of lessons. Years later, when we all moved away from home and would come home on the weekends, I would be the only one that would play.
