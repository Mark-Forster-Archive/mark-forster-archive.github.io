AF1/AF2 Combination

  • There've been several posts by Seraphim and myself about the possibility of using AF1 and AF2 in combination. This was something that I tried out during the development phase, but I never succeeded in overcoming certain problems. There were two main ones:

    1) How do you know when to switch between AF1 and AF2 and back again? You could simply have them as options which you chose between as and when you feel like doing one rather than the other. But I would have preferred a more elegant solution - one which was built into the system itself, rather than depending on how you happen to feel at the time.

    2) The other problem was how to overcome the disadvantages of AF1 mode, such as hit and miss dismissal, slowness of moving through the list, and difficulty of dealing with urgent items.

    When I was doing the testing I never came to a satisfactory answer to these questions and in the end decided that the combination had no advantages over AF2 on its own. But as so often happens, I've just realised that something I thought of in order to solve a completely different problem has solved this one as well.

    I had been thinking in terms of using the AF1 dismissal process, mainly because at that stage I hadn't developed the AF2 dismissal process. But it's just occurred to me that if I use the AF2 dismissal process the problem may well be solved.

    So here's what I'm trying out (and you are welcome to join me but please bear in mind that it hasn't been tested yet):

    1) "Change AF mode" becomes a recurring task on the list. You change from AF1 to AF2 or vice versa when the task stands out, and the task is then re-entered in the normal manner. When you change to AF1 mode you always start from the first active page.

    2) The day starts in the way indicated for AF2 (drawing the line, dismissal if necessary, reading through the whole list). Then you continue in whichever mode you were in when you left off the previous day. To start off initially I recommend AF1 mode.

    3) There is one very important amendment to AF1. There is no AF1 type dismissal. So if you do no tasks on a page you just move on to the next page.

    What I am hoping is that this will improve the ability of AF2 to deal with tasks in the centre of the list, which is the main problem I think it has. It might also make it more tolerant of task dumping. If it works we will be one giant step nearer the perfect task management system!

  • I'm still trying a hybrid that works as follows:

    - AF1 dismissal rules (which, I think, Mark should patent)

    - AF1 is the "default" mode of processing

    - Whenever you get that anxious / lost feeling because you just KNOW something urgent is buried in your list, and you have several things pressing on your mind but you can't work on them now because they are three pages ahead... you know it's time to switch to AF2 mode. So, you add a new task "Return to AF1 mode", jump to the last active page, and start working in AF2 mode.

    - When I say "work in AF2 mode", here is what I mean:
    - Work through the tasks backwards till you find one that stands out.
    - When you finish a task, start again on the last page, and work backwards again.
    - Do not do any "dismissing" when you are in AF2 mode.

    - Eventually, things will calm down again, and "Return to AF1 mode" will stand out. When that happens, just start working from the current page. Optionally, you can start from the first active page; or read through all your tasks and then start from the first active page; or whatever. But I like just starting from the current page.

    I have always had a little plastic red sticky to mark my last page. I now have two of them, one marked "AF1" and another marked "AF2". I use the appropriate one, depending on what mode I'm in, so I don't forget and just go to what I'm used to (AF1).

    So far, I really like it. I started in AF1, switched to AF2 for a bit, then back to AF1, and now am in AF2 mode.

    One thing I really like is that I can "hit the panic button" any time I want, and switch to AF2. But I can't switch to AF1 until that particular task "stands out". So it preserves some of the original AF psychology to help prompt when to switch, but also gives you a way out of an anxious situation.

    YMMV, of course. :-)
