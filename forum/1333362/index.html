  • I prefer the flow of AF1 compared with AF4 Revised.

    Is there value in using AF1 and coding items as (N) (R) (U)?

    Proposed benefits: 1. AF1 seems like a more elegant sytem compared with AF4R.

    2. It's still possible to audit / snap shot the number of each item type.

    3. It's still possible to dismiss items at the New stage before getting into a commitment and / or taking on too many things at once.

    By the way, my use of 'New' would basically be for those items at a 'project level' and above that have not been initiated at all.

    Has anyone already tried this?

  • For sporadic use, AF1 works better than AF4. The fixed size of the closed list (one page) is more consistent. It also works better with "Finally! I can tear out this page.")

    Until I wrote the next bit, and realized I couldn't balance the pros/cons columns, I thought AF1 with NRU pages would work well. Now I think the "different" is actually breaking one AF1's key benefits.

    I do Recurring tasks fairly quickly. I've already evaluated them many times before. Unfinished take a bit longer, since many are long-term, low-commitment projects that get dropped when the week gets busy, but I have invested enough time that I don't want to dismiss them. New take even longer and are the ones most at risk of dismissal.

    Under AF1, there are a few long-lived New tasks on each page. Under AF1 with N/R/U they'd all be on one page.

    Under the original AF1, as a page nears dismissal we have only a few tasks to choose from if we want to keep it open. That can lead to admitting we won't do any of them (good) or finally doing the tough one (good), or picking an unimportant one just so we can keep the page open without doing the tough one (bad).

    With AF1-NRU, there's a much longer list to choose from. It can take many passes before we have to deal with the tough one (bad).

    The organized part of me wants to separate Recurring from New and Unfinished. Recurring often have a short cycle so are written often. It feels like they waste space on the page. They're usually more important, so I want them visible.

    The part that gets things done likes the effect of a few remaining things on the page.

  • So Seraphim's approach is here:
    http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/1330080

    I've taken a rather different approach. I've been gradually grouping things by "project" (i.e. anything that has more than one task, such as designing a rocket, cleaning my house, people to do stuff with, working out at the gym). When I find a task fits in a "project", I may move it in. But I also leave many tasks loose. In this approach:

    - Recurring tasks are in with a project, not in a Recurring list.
    - Unfinished tasks are in Active projects.
    - New stuff is generally on a New page.
    - There is no Old (that's an AF4 concept).
    - When I finish current tasks in a project, I send the project to an Inactive Projects section.

    I run SAF over New and Active pages together, and my own system to reactivate inactive tasks.
