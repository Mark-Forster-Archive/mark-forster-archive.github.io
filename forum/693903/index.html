Work vs. Home discrepancies / experience

  • It seems like I'm not the only one who is finding that their experience of AF at work vs. home is quite different. I'm hoping to stimulate some discussion around that.

    For instance, I have many more open items on my home list than on my work list - this seems to be the norm, but doesn't make logical sense since the average person spends more time at work than at home, or at least about equal.

    A lot of us have problems getting the book out at home - is this just because we're tired and don't want to do more work?

    I think a lot of us don't write down little things that we do at home but we will at work?

    I'm curious to know what the breakdown of home vs. work tasks are for people who have only one book - do they have more home tasks, thus are spending more time doing home stuff than they are working?

    If someone with only one book that works at home comes to a page or pages where they have left only a bunch of home tasks, do they do a bunch of personal work even though it's their "work hours"? Or do they skip pages or dismiss? I can't see how this would work.

    I don't know Christine, I am coming more and more to the realization that it has nothing to do with any system (although I'm far more motivated and get far more done with AF at home than any other methods I've tried - and I've tried ALOT and navel gazed more than is good for you), it's that you can't fit a 2 ton rock into a gopher hole. And there's no magic wand or system that will change that.

    For example, if I was at work and I was super busy and never really getting all my work done, I would probably just stay later. I'd also look into figuring out what I could stop doing, how to move to doing things quarterly instead of monthly, or annually instead of quarterly, or find a way to do things more efficiently. Pawning things off on another group, lots of options... But you still have that option of putting in some overtime at the expense of your home life.

    However, most people can't say "I'm really behind in my laundry, so I can't come to work today." There are still lots of options to reduce the amount of work at home - batch cooking, lowering housekeeping standards (heheh), getting kids to help out etc.

    But you're still looking at a situation where the backlog cannot go away because it is literally too much work for the time available - full stop.

    To be honest, I feel pretty productive even on those days at home where I do AF "wrong" too, whether that's picking the least resisted item, not because it really stood out, skipping a page or even hopping around (only a little bit :-) and treating the whole list like one big closed list - just because I feel like it - because I'm always - always - coming back to the system - which is an achievement in itself.

    Not only that, but the slight depression that has dogged me for years is gone and I don't feel like having a glass of wine every day when I get home anymore. I don't think it's a coincidence that this happened at the same time as starting AF. I believe that the brain does release happy endorphins when you're engaged, working on something and making progress.
