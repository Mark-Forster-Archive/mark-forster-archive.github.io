  • I'm happily using Omnifocus. I keep things simple: one list, with a task that is just a typed line (--------) as, well, "the line" between the open or active and closed or backlog lists.

    When I get to the situation where I have done a complete pass of the Closed list without doing any of the tasks, then, instead of "highlighting" all those remaining tasks, I assign them a context called "Review". (I don't use any other contexts. They simply don't make any sense for the way I work.) And I then enter a new line at the end of the Active list, which now becomes the new Closed list.

    The next time I cycle back to the beginning of the list, those "highlighted" ("Review") tasks are abandoned, re-entered, or put into a reminder system as appropriate.

    If I need to keep track of where I am on a list, I put a dot (option-8) before the task I'm currently working on.

    I use the "Clean Up" command to instantly delete completed tasks. (Cheap thrill!) Deleted tasks are archived automatically so I can always go back and review them if I want to.

    Things I love about Omnifocus:

    The interface is clean and elegant. I can keep things simple and uncluttered. If I need a note (a URL, a phone number) there's a jolly little drop down note area that then disappears.

    New tasks enter at the bottom of the list, just like my old paper list.

    I can keep one list, and it's always with me, and it's always up-to-date. I sync between my laptop and my iPhone via MobileMe and that syncing ability is a dream come true for me.

    When I think of something I need to do, out comes the phone and on the list it goes. No more lost slips of papers, emails to myself to process, or winging it because "the list" is in the OTHER notebook which is at the OTHER work site.

    Omnifocus isn't cheap but I got a substantial educational discount. For me, it's the second best thing I've ever done for my productivity and peace-of-mind. (Autofocus being the best thing!)

    I have many (paper) project files, and a lot of my project planning is done in those files but I also keep project folders in Omnifocus and I use those jotting down ideas and thinking about the project that I then mesh with the paper files.

    [BTW, and completely off topic, I use a very simplified version of Martin Tarnouth's management system which he wrote about on Edward Tufte's wonderful blog. Every project, no matter how large or small, gets a clear plastic slipcase folder and a cover sheet with its title and I make progress notes on the cover sheet. I also color code all my files, calendar entries, notebooks, etc: orange for teaching, green for making (art) and blue for life.]

  • Here is how I do it. I have three projects of type "Single Actions" (icons are boxes): Backlog, Active, Someday. Someday has status "On hold", it's for stuff that I may do some day but not currently doing.

    My view setup is:
    Ungrouped, Unsorted, Available actions, Any duration, Any flag state.

    When I'm working on the back log, I click the Backlog project, and work on things as per instruction. Any thing that is finished gets crossed out. Any thing that needs further work is duplicated (cmd-d), the original one is crossed out, the new one is dragged to the Active project.

    When I'm working on the active list, I do the same except that I leave the duplicated task in place. Regularly I select every task (cmd-a), right-click, and sort -> date added. This way re-added task go to the end.

    When I go through the backlog without doing anything and need to review it, I select all, right-click, set flag. I then take every action from the Active project and put them in the Backlog project. Next time I work on the back log I review every flagged task, discarding them (delete or put in Someday) or re-entering them in the active list if necessary.

    When I think of new tasks I enter them using the quick input panel, it automatically adds them at the end.

    I think that's about it. I works quite well. If you have any question, don't hesitate to ask away.

  • I'd prefer to work on paper, but I've found OF has some powerful advantages. I'm very project focused, with lots of deadline-driven projects, and I need an electronic record of what I've done. Most of my information arrives electronically, and I find that writing down a phone number in a task list, and then when the task is completed re-recording the number in my database, is overhead I can do without.

    OmniFocus works very well for combining these requirements with AF4.

    My OF setup currently looks like this:

    - Projects filed in two folders: "Live" (actively working on) and "Stack" (everything else).

    - Three contexts: "Open", "Closed" and "About". Open & Closed are, of course, my open and closed lists. "About" is short for "Out & About" and has reminders of stuff to get or do when I'm in town.

    - Perspectives to fit my work habits. My main operating perspective shows a flat list of tasks sorted by Date Added, with the newest at the top (in a deadline driven job, this works better than the paper default of the newest at the bottom).
    Within this perspective I can look at contexts/lists separately, or all my tasks at once if I click on the main "Contexts" container.

    With a press of a hotkey I can also switch to a list of projects with their respective tasks arrayed beneath each project. That allows me to quickly survey what's ahead, what's missing and what's redundant to each project. This isn't as easy in the paper version.

    It's an easy matter to drag tasks between open and closed lists, and easy to enter them into the right project and right task list in the first place - one of the beauties of OF.

    The currently active task gets a flag.

    In a future, less complicated life, I'd like to have my life contained within the battered covers of a leather organiser covered in travel stains and memories. I could do that now, with determination, but it would be far less efficient than what I've got. I don't think OmniFocus can be bettered as an electronic solution.
