Procrastination : Does GTD & Todo-lists cause it?


  • As you know, *Procrastination* is relative and very subjective thing.
    Since how you do know when you're procrastinating?

    Example.
    If you feel that should write report, but you're reading emails;
    Question is: do you procrastinate?
    Trivial answer: yes, you're procrastinating.
    Alternative answer:: no, i'm not procrastinating - i need to read these emails too

    So, in a way, procrastination is created within our minds. And you can get things done
    still while you're procrastinating. You could have todo list of 5 tasks. And you
    've done 4 of those, however, you feel lousy and think you're ineffective since you haven't done task 5. On the other hand, you're effective and you have not procrastinated since you have already done 4 items...

    So, procrastination is subjective and could be defined as "not doing something what you think you SHOULD be doing".

    And now comes tricky part...
    When you're "collecting stuff" and writing large to-do-lists => you feel always that you're procastinating (at least something) because there is always some tasks that you haven't done.

    You've put your finger very neatly on the major problem with time management which I have been attempting to address with my alternative systems:

    With "Get Everything Done", the idea is to move forward systematically on everything that it currently active. That way you may not yet have finished everything, but at least you know that you are moving forward with everything.

    With "Do It Tomorrow", the idea is that you keep your commitments audited so that your work is in balance with the time available to complete it.

    With "Autofocus", the idea is to give a framework for your intuition to work with your rational mind to select what should be done.

    With my new super top secret system, the idea is that everything gets done as soon as it needs to be so you never have any sense of procrastination.

  • Hi Mark,

    I like what you said; one should get done everything.

    Yes, i know that this thinking goes *against* traditional time management, since nearly all the systems always make assumptions like: you can't do everything, you need to prioritize etc.
    However, the problem is, that if you take attitude "i need to prioritize or i can't do everything" => then one is possibly feeling procrastination because one is not doing some of the to-do items (because of prioritization).

    Basicly, i'd recommend this type of system:

    1.) write down all things you need to get done (this is common sense and like in GTD)
    2.) do rough estimation how long each item potentiaaly requires time to be done
    3.) sum up all time needed to get whole list done

    Now comes interesting part: even if you have tons of tasks and complex projects,
    usually the cumulative time needed is not in fact very big

    Example; you have 100 five minute tasks => 100 x 5 = 500 => less than 9 hours!
    These 5 minute tasks are straight forward actions like; reply email, call x, search web, write, draft picture, buy, read, fix etc.

    So, your task list could (at least theoretically) be completed in *one* workday or so.
    Next,

    4.) now you have cumulative "time cost" of your to-do list
    5.) If don't have possibility to complete all in one or two days, you can block three "3 hour timeboxes" spread over next three days
    6.) when you have complete task, mark how much time it took in reality (this way you get better in estimating "time cost" of tasklist.
    7.) do tasks :-)
