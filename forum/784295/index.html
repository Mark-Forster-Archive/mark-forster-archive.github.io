Does one's age matter in time management?

  • I assume that most persons reading and/or contributing to this Discussion Forum are in search of the Holy Grail of time management. I'm also guessing that if a poll were carried out about time management the age distribution of the pollsters, statistically, would not show as a normal distribution curve.

    My question to Mark and everybody else too, would be: Has anyone studied the impact of or attitude to time management according to people's age? In extreme terms, to illustrate the question, I would think that a teenager's attitude to time management may be different than that of, say, an octogenarian's. Therefore, perhaps a different time management system would be appropriate for each group, with age being a critical indicator of the appropriate system in each case.

  • Funny to look at the two extremes, teens and octogenarian. I think a psychologist's view might be too look at a personality dimension like conscientiousness which is a stable trait for most people and seems to predict the level of organisation an individual possesses (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conscientiousness). Then you might speculate, the higher on the scale of conscientiousness the more likely the individual will be productive/organised and thus be using timemanagement principles.

    On the other hand, when I look at my self, I never became so heavily interested in timemanagement and productivity because of peer influences or fashion, early or later. Few of my (older) friends have any serious interest in it. On a psychologist's personality&intelligence test I used to score pretty low on the 'conscientious' dimension. I was naturally unorganised but once I got out of high school and then add another year or two until I got a sense of what direction I wanted to go in, I found it critical that I became proficient in timemanagement and productivity. If you have ambitious goals and you're not effective and efficient with your time you'll feel a burning desire to learn how, which is what happened with me. And if you're also successful in attaining ambitious goals the more likely you are to be using effective time management methods.

    In high school I didn't really have goals that required me to be effective with my time, I was just going with the semi-obligatory flow of things. Once I got goals that required me to be effective, I developed an interest in productivity systems. So you might say the use of particular timemanagement methods depend most heavily on the type of goals you have, regardless of age. (and even if you don't have consciously chosen goals, you still have goals in the way of having to achieve outcomes in whatever profession you're in)

    When you approach a octogenarian's age you're just more likely to have learnt all you're open to learn in a lifetime in terms of productivity and timemanagement. Too bad there wasn't any AF in the 50s. At least there was structured procrastination!

  • Kids here in Canada are started off with an agenda / daytimer for school when they start grade 1. It has homework due, what to study that night etc. The parents are expected to sign off on it every day - it seems to be a good method to get them started in basic time management.

    I started using a daytimer in my early 20's when going to University and would schedule everything in there. There isn't a lot of other demands on your time at that age and usually you're more spontaneous and not necessarily working towards any kind of goals and don't have multiple priorities to juggle.

    I started becoming interested in self-development and time management in my late 20's and early 30's because I realized there were a lot of dreams I had had for my life that would never be achieved if I didn't work at them in a concerted way. That's when I started scheduling all of my time and started working on my goals using a variety of methods. Unfortunately, I was quite rigid and easily discouraged if things didn't work out exactly as planned - so at that age, I hadn't learned yet to be flexible.

    In your 30's is when most people's careers are their focus, so they need something to manage that. But it seems most people I know don't manage much outside of their work lives apart from reminders of when to show up at events. Most of my friends are in their 30's and I hear a lot of "call me and remind me". Most people I've worked with in the last 10 years have been in this age group as well and they don't seem to manage their time using any method other than writing down or putting in their computer deadline dates. I have worked quite a lot with engineers though, and they appear to be the exception.

  • Hi Mark,

    Yes, I agree that it does depend on the school. I had lunch with a teacher who was moving to a new school. He was shocked at the rigid discipline ... it seemed to him that it would be an awful environment ... it was almost militaristic. But then he taught a "test" class for his job interview, and was surprised at how attentive the kids were. They focused on what he was saying and tracked him has we walked around the room. They had their notes out and were paying attention. So he started to think seriously about the efficacy of stern discipline. I guess it depends on a lot of things working together.

    Re: Poem. I'm certainly no memory expert (nor do I play one on TV ;-) If I were to set up such a thing I would start in the earliest years and "teach" the laws of memory ... not as a lesson but as a game. I'd have kids try to remember a short list of things then show them a better way. I'd use one "law" at a time. Probably I'd start with exaggeration. "How can we remember 'shoe'? Suppose we imagine a big shoe, one 10 times the size of a house?" Each day I'd do a little more, just as a game. Then I'd start applying it to what they were studying. "How can you remember that Colombus discovered America? (Assuming you have to teach that myth! ;-) Can you see a big ship landing on America and there all these Indians waiting then a gigantic yellow school BUS drives off the ship? What kind of bus? A school bus? NO! It's actually a COLOMBUS!" Something along those lines. I'm sure if I actually had to do it I'd come up with something better ;-)

    As to memorizing a poem, I don't know the "best" way, but any way is better than telling the kid they have to come in tomorrow with it memorized! I would start by explaining the poem ... I think it is easier to memorize what makes sense. Personally, I'd start at the end and work backward to the beginning. I'd learn it one line at a time. But first I'd identify "trigger" words for each line ... especially the rhyming words. Once you know those you have it mostly beat.

    One thing I'd be sure to do is use all sensory channels. I'd read it, say it out loud, write it out, etc. Then when I though I had it, I'd "perform" it. IOW, poetry is an art ... it is meant to come alive. There are rhythms and cadences to play with. All of this helps and taken together removes the boredom of repetitive learning, if nothing else.

    I have more experience with NOT learning things as I was awful in school. I'd be tagged as having ADHD, in today's schools ... but in my day they called me a "problem". Oh well. But at least I know what does not work. I only really got my memory under control when in my 20's I took a memory course and found that there was a system to these things ... it was not just a matter of repeating things over and over until you went mad.
