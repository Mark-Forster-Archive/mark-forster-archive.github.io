Procrastination not confined to unpleasant tasks

  • I hope it isn't old news on this forum, but I found the article at http://www.insideinfluence.com/inside-influence-report/2010/11/the-persuasive-pull-of-procrastination.html thought provoking on the subject of procrastination.

    "...we associate procrastination with tasks that are less pleasant to us. But an interesting new series of studies points out that often we are just as likely to put off activities that we find attractive and enjoyable.

    ... People might have a tendency to put off not just less desirable tasks but desirable ones too ... one reason why this might be the case is the fact that people typically believe that they will have more time in the future to carry out enjoyable activities but fail to realize that they will likely be just as busy in the future. As a result they simply end up not completing the activity."

  • David,

    First, I think that what a lot of Mark's systems do--and why they are good--is create a sense of urgency. If I feel that I have an abundance of time, I can put things off. If my list tells me that time is about to run out, I feel compelled by urgency.

    This flies in the face of Coveyesque "urgency vs. importance" classifications. But I personally find it helpful to trick myself into making stuff urgent. I think that that is part of the reason why I like DWM so much.

    There is another study that associates happiness with minds that are focused on present tasks and unhappiness with minds that wander:

    http://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2010/11/101111141759.htm

    Presumably, if I could reduce the amount that my mind wanders, I could increase my happiness. I want to increase my happiness. So, how can I reduce the amount my mind wanders?

    I have not seen the original study. But it seems possible that one can be focused by doing little and often. That is, one can wander from task to task with high frequency, as long as one is focused on whatever is the current task at hand.

    I am going to try to be super-strict about only doing things after I write them down. I'll see if that keeps me more focused and happier.

    Meanwhile, the larger picture states that I want a task system that:

    keeps me organized
    keeps me focused
    keeps me feeling a sense of urgency
