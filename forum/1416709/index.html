Velocity through the pages and tracking bigger projects

  • So far SFv3 is working great for me but the nature of my work is keeping me from using it as intended. The general problem is i can only do 1 to 1.5 pages a day. So it takes me 3-4 days to loop through the complete set of pages. Here is what happens:

    1. Nature of my work is 80% it all comes from client requests that have to be completed in a timely manner. But they can be big time consumers. Dismissing them is not an option. So that puts a lot of pressure on column 1 items.

    2. New stuff comes in at a steady clip. Urgent promises to do it today go in the current col2, urgent stuff I know I probably can't do today I've been sticking on col2 of the next page, if I happen to get to that page that's cool, and it helps pull me forward. None urgent stuff goes into column 1. So far so good.

    3. Working the list I tend to spend a lot of time scanning the current col2 to pick something to work on, then while the status bars run I start scanning the list for the next item to queue up or to multitask on (we do computer system management so there are lots of pokey tasks). The beauty of sfv3 is my peripheral vision tends to pick off column 1 items I am avoiding and as it turns out starting and those are often better "filler" tasks to do while waiting on the next step of an urgent one.. So far still so good. This keeps me from running out of fresh column 2 space. Unfortunately many of these col 2 tasks can take 1-2 hrs to complete.

    So as stated at the start, the only problem is the velocity through the pages is awful. It is really not the the tasks I write dowe are too big, they really are naturally time consuming, and big, but SFv3 is still handling them.. I need an artificial way to break them apart without loosing track of the bigger picture.....

    I think I can improve my results if I do this:

    A. Treat my long tasks as "projects" ala GTD.
    B. Enter these projects in column 1.
    C. When I hit the project in col1 and decide to take action on it, think of the next discrete action (preferably making it artificially small) enter that single next action in col 2, cross off the project in column 1, and renter the project in column 1. Then process col 2 in the normal fashion.

    That change might allow me to have column 2 tasks that can be completed in just one or two pages turns and a lot less duration, and reentering the projects right away in column 1 ensures that I can easily keep track of the larger goals that might take a week or two to complete. Additionally it gives me an easy way to set aside big projects when the urgent customer driven stuff gets high volume.

  • I've never worked in IT so it's difficult for me to advise, but it strikes me that the problem is related to not working "little and often" enough.

    When I'm doing a large writing project (which I guess is somewhat similar to a large IT project in the way it can be handled), I write in fairly short bursts. So Project X would be broken up X.... X..... X.....X and so on until finished.

    In SuperFocus terms that means that X is constantly moving round the list in the Column 2s of successive pages until it is completed.

    When you have several large projects you have a choice of either completing Project X, then completing Project Y and then Project Z, or of doing a bit of X, a bit of Y, a bit of Z and alternating them.

    The first method (finishing each project in turn) is the most efficient and the quickest, but may not always be possible.

    I think you also need to look at your definition of urgent. For SuperFocus purposes "urgent" means "need to be working on it on the page I'm currently on". You may need to keep a list of times when various projects become urgent, so you can enter them in Column 2 at an appropriate time.

  • If everything is urgent, then nothing's urgent.

    That's because priority is about the order in which you do things. If you give everything the same priority then you are no further forward in deciding what order to do them in.

    The degree of urgency you give tasks or projects is related to the order in which you are going to do them. As I said in my earlier post it is more efficient and quicker to do projects in turn than to attempt to do them all at once.

    This is because if you do XYZXYZXYZ you finish Project X, Y and Z at approximately the same time.

    But if you do XXXYYYZZZ you finish Project X four sessions before you would in the previous example, Y two sessions before you would in the previous example, and Z at the same time as you would in the previous example.
