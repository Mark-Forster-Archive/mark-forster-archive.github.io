    My tagging process for tasks is mainly used to track series of tasks though the pages, as well as providing reference information for these tasks. After using Autofocus for a while, I found that the "little and often" approach resulted in a dramatic increase in my productivity. But since I work in a project-intensive environment with tons of discretionary time, I wanted a way to track how often I was working on a task series or project.

    On the task sheet ( http://files.me.com/jeremy.benz/z0p3vk ) the far left column is for the origination tag, expressed as a page number and a line letter. So, on Page 5 (after I've gone through every available task with no intention of doing it), Line B, I get to "Check News Feeds." It stands out to me, so I place my blue post-it arrow under it (like the "dot") and fire up Google Reader.

    I pat myself on the back and mark the task complete with a "/" through the box, meaning that I intend to do this task again. In the area to the right of the box, I fill in the current date (just the day number if the current month is the same as the month the sheet was created on), and under that I put the destination tag - the next unfilled line of my last active page (lets say 10Q). I am able to flip between my current task and my last active page with the divider and post-it arrow.

    I then flip to my last active page (Page 10), and fill in the origination tag with "5B" since that was the task that motivated the entry for this one. I then fill in "Check News Feeds" since I will be doing it again. Now, by following the origination tag, I can see the last time I performed that task, which allows the tracking of the task series. Basically, this links related tasks together. I do this whenever I reenter tasks, or move though the next actions of my projects.

    This also works very well for projects, where you have a series of next actions that you can track through the pages. I have a basic project list in Evernote that I print and punch whenever changes are made which Includes the Project number, Title, and the start tag where the first action of the project is listed. When the project is completed, I enter the tag for the final task and move the whole string to my Completed Projects List.

    I use the second column from the left to include reference tags like project number, email, paper file location, etc. These tags exist as reference items for the task: where to get information to help me complete it. They are not contexts (like @fax): I feel contexts detract from the "standing out" portion of task selection that makes Mark's system so revolutionary.

    The back of a page is reserved for notes associated with the task, 90% of the time, they are notes that I write down during task execution or after the completion of it. The presence of digital notes is captured in Evernote, which supports tags and fits well within the system (I can drop 5B into Evernote to look at the news I clipped into it as a result of the task I completed, for example).

    For me, Autofocus does great job of sifting time-sensitive items. I have a tickler file for paper items (43 folders) and my iCal for electronic items. Most often, I will schedule a time sensitive item to hit my AF notebook a week before it is due (I usually can run through 3-5 cycles of my active pages in a week). Following Mark's suggestion of using a 3x5 card as a bookmark to hold urgent items that must be completed that day, I use a Levenger Tasker Sheet, which serves the same purpose, but is "circa friendly."

    That's how I currently do things with Autofocus. I realize the tagging takes more time and detracts from the system's elegant simplicity, but I feel I get more out of the bargain in the ability to easily track what I am doing. I could just as easily throw the Circa into the bottom of drawer and go back to my Moleskine (or go 100% digital with Andreas's autofocus.cc ) if this tagging business gets too complicated. ;-)
