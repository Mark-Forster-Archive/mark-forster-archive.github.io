'Stand Out' items & waiting to be released

  • For starters: So far, my experience of AF is increasingly positive; and as a relative novice to time management approaches who has a history of chaotic working practices, that's saying something.

    In skimming over the forum posts, there seems to be a limited amount of discussion about the process of choosing which 'stand out' task to work on (in fact, there seems far more discussion in the type of notebook one should use. 33 lined A4 hardback notebook from Tesco for 80p is my weapon of choice, by the way).

    I'm aware that Mark advises not to "prioritise items mentally" but to "wait for a feeling of release." I'm also aware that he indicates it is a practice that is easier to do than it is to describe. I'm wondering, however, whether it comes easier to those of you who have arrived at AF with a strong background in time management techniques, and therefore are more attuned to this elusive "feeling of release" and less prone to mentally prioritise without meaning to?

    One of Moises' posts mentioned his sense of feeling burdened to complete tasks on one page, because he was desperate to get to the page upon which he knew he had something urgent to do. I can identify with some of this desperation, although I recognise one has to (a) use one's common sense to override the system at times of significant urgency, and (b) gradually grow to trust the system over time.

    I also wonder how much of the 'feeling of release' may come easier to some personality types, and less to others?

    Any old hands or fellow-novices want to offer their opinion?

I would class myself as creative and intuitive by nature but worked in an analytical environment, solving problems, where I believe creativity in looking for solutions gave an edge.

    I have looked at various time management and other professional development systems and had no problem understanding the concept of the "feeling of release" BUT it took me a long time to actually get how it worked in practice, The fact that I had already worked through that barrier I believe gave me a real head start in using AF.

    It is incredibly hard to explain, the closest I can come to it is saying it is the item you "feel" like doing. Our rational mind says "well that's ridiculous, of course I will feel like doing "fun" things but I will never "feel" like doing my tax return, or that client report or cleaning the car. Those are things that just have to be done". In practice however that does not happen to the extent we believe it will. Inexplicably we will "feel" like doing many of those "non-fun" things, and those that we don't will cause us to question those tasks and their place in our lives.

    Nevertheless there will always be some things that we will not want to do but must do and that does need to be kept in mind. I cannot imagine ever "feeling" that I want to go to the dentist, but it must be done. It can take a while to learn to go with what you feel but it gets easier with practice.

  • Hi folks!

    No idea what INTJ, ENTJ, Myers-Briggs, etc. even means but I'll have a bash at answering Neumatist's query about "standing out"!

    I found that if I substituted "catches my attention" for "stands out" then it was very clear which task to do - the one that MOST caught my attention as I scanned the list the second, slower time. Often this may be a task that I really don't like the idea of but when I scanned, it jumped out more than any of the others and shouted "DO ME FIRST!!".

    As the list on each page gets smaller this feeling of dread at starting an outstanding task heightens but does go away when I think to myself 'if I just do the tiniest thing on it' such as 'get out the file' or 'look up xyz on the interweb', then the resistance to do the task fades. Also I often find that when I've made that start then somehow by magic it's finished in one go as it was the thought of starting it pre-Autofocus that was most holding me back. Of course, sometimes there are tasks on my list that I'm never going to 'want' to do but even these become much easier if I chip away at them bit by tiny bit - sometimes rephrasing the task to make it less threatening can also help. E.g. instead of 'do project X' I might have to rephrase it to 'begin project X' which isn't nearly so scary.

    If I have two or more tasks that jump out then I just compare them with each other and see which one 'feels' like it should be done first, or even which I feel like doing first. In a way it doesn't matter as they'll all get done eventually. If one is much more urgent than another and the consequences of not doing it are fairly dire then you again have answered your question as to which to do.

    I hope this helps...
