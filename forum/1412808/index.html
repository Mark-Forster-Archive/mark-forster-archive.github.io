SF helped me get more done...

  • ...in my one week vacation than I have in YEARS. Maybe not volume-wise overall, but things that I'd put off. Things I needed to get done.

    Thanks so much Mark!

    I've used OmniFocus, paper, OF again, RTM, OF again. I always spent way more time admiring and tinkering with the software than actually doing anything. I knew that I worked better from a paper list and often tried printing things, or writing things down from a larger system. But I always got bogged down in trying to 'improve' the system.

    I've cleaned out boxes, bags, drawers of things that have sat around ignored forever. I still have more to go, but I'm confident that it'll get done sooner rather than later.

    I've left things in OF. I haven't pulled anything out of it to put into SF. It's still sitting there waiting for me. And when I feel I can/need to, I'll go through and pull some things in. I'd thought that I'd continue along with GTD overall and just use SF as my 'do' part. Now, I don't know. It'll be interesting to see. I'm sure I recognize some participants on this site from GTD sites. I'm up for any thoughts others have on this.

    One amazing thing to me is somehow this week has empowered me to start the little, baby steps to begin a project that I've always felt would be very daunting...I have a car that has been sitting in the garage for years and I want to revive it. I always thought I'd need to be ready to 'do the project'. But so far I've found the records on the car, found the books about it, found some resources online, began conversing with some 'experts' online about how best to proceed. My goal is to get it going by spring break. Not 'done' just going. I thought it would sit for several more years. I'm confident that I'll be able to tackle it in bite-sized chunks and get it done. AND, most importantly, not neglect all the other areas in my life that need attention (which is what I tend to do, bury myself in one project/part of my life to the detriment of all others).

    Thanks. Thanks. Thanks.

  • The most noticeable thing about SF for me has been the way in which an urgent task impacts existing tasks in C2.

    Take this scenario: C1 is full with just one task left, and I am working intently on a project in C2. An urgent task commands my attention which I duly enter to C2. The project I was working on goes away to C2 on the next page. I deal with the urgent task and am eager to get back to my project. But I can't leave the page until the task in C1 is actioned.

    It's an uneasy position to be in. Obviously, the last task in C1 is one which I have avoided working on (that's why its the last task), but I am now forced to deal with it before I earn the right to go back to my project.

    It gets the job done, no doubt.
