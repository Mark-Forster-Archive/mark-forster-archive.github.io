Slow Movers and Fast Movers

  • I am moving SLOWLY through the lists. There are days I only touch two or three tasks on it. I very often start something and drown in it for hours. So, in AF1 days, my active pages remained open for months, and only when I reached the end of the list - where the urgent tasks where - I was up to date and responding. I always postponed the "plunge" back to the first active page, because I knew I would be away from the end page with the hot topics for at least a week... unless I cheated, which I, of course, did.

    Mark Forster himself is, without a doubt, a FAST mover. We all could see this when he introduced AF4, moving through his list several times in one day. As he explained, he was passing through his AF1 lists at least one time a day as well. We saw him completing a lot of tasks and touching even more. The point is, I simply can't work this way.

    What I would like to discuss is whether this might make a difference in regard of which task management system works for a certain person.

    For example, if you pass your whole list at least one time a day, you come across each task at least once: So you're less likely to miss a task that has become urgent. On the other hand, if you keep your tasks pending for a longer time, it's more likely they won't get dismissed too soon, but attacked when the right moment has come.

    Is, for example, AF4 more suited for fast movers, while AF1 or AF3 is more for slow movers?

  • AndreasE said;
    <<And some tasks demand preparation: You can't just get up from your chair, walk down into your garden and lay one brick on your wall-in-progress - you have to change clothes, you have to prepare mortar... >>

    I have had problems with this aspect for some of my projects in the past.

    I sculpt in clay and wax. I paint with acrylics. I also make puppets out of all kinds of materials. And I enjoy making electronics gadgets and experiments. But the preperation time and the tidy-up time for these was long - and that was preventing me switching from one to the other effeciently.

    So I set about reducing these 'tool-up' times. I keep several such projects 'on the go' in my 'workshop' (a corner of a shed).

    I have found materials and techniques that prevent things 'going off' between my bursts of activity. And (most important of all) I have developed a system for keeping everything I need for each project all in one place so I can switch from one to the other in a few moments.

    I bought a stack of baker's trays (one metre square ply-wood boards with a rim). I keep all my tools and materials for each project on one of these. I built a stacking system that allows me to simply slide the tray into the rack when not in use. So my tiny workshop is no longer cluttered.

    So when I get the urge to do a little sculpting or whatever - I pull out the baker's tray with my latest work-in-progress (and all the tools and materials), pop it on my bench, setup my lamp, and away I go in seconds.

    Before it would take so long to find the work, the tools, the materials etc. And the tidy-up afterwards was a pain - so it never happened. And as a result I avoided these projects for months at a stretch.

    These days, when I get home from the office, I can get around to two or three of these projects before bedtime. Life is so much more enjoyable. And I dont watch TV all evening anymore.
