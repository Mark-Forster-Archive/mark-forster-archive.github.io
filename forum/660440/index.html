Comfort Zones

  • Hi all,

    I like AF. It's been working well for me since the start of the beta test. Like many others here I've found things going more smoothly and have taken action on a number of things that were stalled.
    However, nothing I have done has so far drawn me beyond the normal boundaries of my life - my job, my friends, my home life, my existing interests.
    This year I am going to need to make some changes that will break down those boundaries and take me completely out of my comfort zone. I've made plans for these new goals and added various actions to my AF list and will see how they get processed.
    I'm now curious - has anyone else here found AF to draw them way our of their comfort zone, or does the ease of choosing and doing tasks from the list tend to stay within the normal boundaries? I'm curious because I wonder if AF would follow our normal mental patterns or lead us to change them.

  • AF can help doing things that you previously had more resistance for. In that light AF makes stuff more comfortable but you'll also be doing more. But something that has come up for me in a previous post was that AF is just a system. What you put in defines what you get out. The incentive to go beyond the current range of experience is dependent on the person. I don't think AF necessarily brings on 'real' growth on its own except higher productivity and the accompanying better feelings. (which are big things in itself, but..)

    Looking at the success stories in the forums I think the theme has been about people achieving things they needed to have done, breaking through procrastination etc. But the things we procrastinate on are often not the most meaningful things in life, a core reason of procrastination.

    I think the downside of using AF isolated from a broader plan or vision which you implement and review regularly, is that it facilitates falling in love with productivity and getting things done. This is part due because it works so well. This is one observation that long term use might give us more confirmation of (or not). The same goes for most other systems though.

    I've always likened GTDers as spinning in a mouse wheel much faster and efficient than an average person. I wonder if AF can express itself as the equivalent to being in the mousewheel but having a smile on our face with much less stress. In other words, a much more comfortable mouse wheel. It's still a mouse wheel. Not meaning this to be a generalisation, but I think it can warrant reflection.

  • Hi Peter

    There are a number of examples I could give but they probably won't seem as significant as the impact they have had. The main benefit has been the ability to look at reasons for resistance. With AF effectively leaving the mind a lot freer to concentrate on things outside of "how to spin the wheel" I have the choice to just to do the high resistance items to get them out of the way, or to examine why I am resisting. If I allow myself to examine the reason I then have a further choice as to whether to act on what I learn.

    Tasks that have had a "meaningful exchange of information with me"?

    Stacking or unstacking my dishwasher - my resistance was down to not wanting to be tied down to a specific time to do the task (true for a number of my domestic chores which are now slotting in quite nicely).

    Preparing a talk (which should have been something I would have enjoyed doing) - the resistance showed me that I needed to give a talk on an entirely different subject (and yes I had the discretion to change it!)

    A major project that was deadlined did not stand out - I identified that my approach to doing it was based on perfectionism and that I needed to change the approach.

    Various tasks that have been high resistance I have seen are linked to fear responses such as fear of conflict.

    Whilst a lot of the tasks themselves have been pretty minor the impact has been massive (and that impact had been largely unrecognised for what it was pre AF). In some ways it is quite overwhelming to face a lot of the things that arise but on the other hand it is incredibly freeing. What I think is so amazing about AF is that the things that are able to be identified are identified so easily, and without any sense of threat or fear. Also I am still in the process of actioning some fairly major changes to my project management system, changes I have never made time for previously, so anticipate an even greater degree of productivity going forward - not just a happy mouse but Super Mouse! :-)

    I really do enjoy our discsussions too - in our busy lives of "doing" how often do we make the time to really think through things? - so thank you too!

    Your comment "this idea of equating doing with value. Don't we measure people based on their achievements all the time in society?" is so very true. Have you noticed that we always ask people "what do you do?" as if what we do defines who we are. Interestingly the reply in most cases is not "I do ......" but "I am....." However when you take that a step further it is normally we ourselves who are actually the ones making that measurement rather than others. How often do we hear people say "I am only ......" or "I am just ...... at the moment" as if apologising for their "position"? (Or do/say that ourselves!)

    I also think you are absolutely right about productivity systems in general. However, the more I think I think about it, I'm actually not sure if that can be said to be true about AF. Let me try to explain that. I think that in life in general we will all of necessity have our lists of things to do, regardless of the complexity of of our lives and/or the complexity of the "things to do". As people "get more important" by conventional standards they are more likely to have people to do a lot of those things for them so that conversely their prestige value switches from "doing" to having "someone else to do". How does that affect identify? Topic for another discussion perhaps :-). However, if you look at the concept that doing = achieving then any system that enables more effective "doing is going to have an impact. Again the ability to do is not bad - it is essential in any context and a very necessary part of our lives. What is a factor though, in the concept that doing = achievement, is in how that belief is linked to the identity of the person "doing the doing" - how that ties in with their own levels of self worth etc.. Any system, particularly the more structured ones, can increase productivity and enable life to be lived more easily and in a more managed or more controlled way. For many, maybe even the vast majority, that will be enough. It satisfies the basic need to keep things in order and manageable, and that is what most people want. But what about those of us who have never found "the perfect system". Is it the fault of the system/s? Or is it that somehow we feel that just "doing" is not enough? That somehow our identity is not just in our list of "things to do"?

    If that makes any sort of sense :-) ....... it is for that reason that I wonder whether that does hold true for AF. Can it be used "just" as a tool for productivity? Perhaps if it is used in a DIT type of way, with each list being treated as a will do list, then maybe. I would imagine that there would still be a degree of resistance to the last few items and that they will be done by the "teeth gritting" method, but yes that would increase productivity. However, if AF is used in the way it is designed to be used, then I think my answer would have to be "no". It is the very way in which AF not only enables you to think about reasons for resistance, but to be used fully, requires that assesment, and for genuine change to be made requires that assesment to lead to action. If we are not prepared to "go that extra step" then the system becomes "just" another productivity tool, and to contine Mark's Rolls Royce analogy, it would be like driving a Rolls Royce and never leaving first gear. It would get you from A to B but what a waste!

    One of the amazing things about AF for me is that it does, when used fully, focus the mind, enabling us to "really" think about things that are important. That for me is the crux of the matter - do I "just" become more productive, or do I allow myself to really think about what I do, and as a result where I am going. I may not know the end of the journey but it sure is exciting! It made me realise why students are often so radical - they are in an environment where they are encouraged to really think. As we get caught up in the minutiaie of our lives - or our "to do lists", I suspect that thinking is one the first things to go - or our thinking time is very specifically directed to our work related tasks rather than our lives in general and perhaps more specifically our own paths in that life.

    The other thing that made me think was your use of the phrase "familiarity zone". It is so true that we get used to what we are familiar with and that becomes our comfort zone, and in effect acts like a security blanket that we want to hang on to at all costs. Even things like illness can become a comfort zone - if I become ill and cannot "do" then I may sit and watch the TV. As I am ill I do not have to feel guilty about doing so. Take away the illness and what comes back? The need to do and the guilt of not doing.

    There's a very interesting story in the Bible where Jesus heals a cripple, but before doing so asks "do you want to be well?". You naturally think, what sort of a question is that? Of course he wants to be well! But just think about what would change in that man's life - his existing daily routine was predictable - he waited to be healed. He was looked after and cared for by friends and family. But if he was healed what then? His whole life would change. He would no longer be reliant on others. He would need to look after himself, to work, to do. Was Jesus not, by His question, pointing out to the man that his healing involved leaving his familiarity zone?

    More food for thought ......... ? :-)
