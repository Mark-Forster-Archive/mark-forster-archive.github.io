  • Hi Karin

    I would really recommend not trying to shorten pages as effectively that can increase the numbers of items you *have* to do before getting to those items you believe are important on a later page (in that you have to do at least as many items as numbers of pages between you and those items), and also reduces the number of passes before the system dismisses the item. If you have 10 lines to a page the maximum number of times that item is looked at is 10 - on a 34 lined page it is 34.

    Remember that you only *have* to do one item per page, and that you can break down tasks. Each time you see "Save world hunger" you are saying above that you just want to see the important tasks by which I think you mean tasks that you believe are urgent (and therefore considered important to you now). It is usually our rational mind that creates that Urgent/Important grading - by working intuitively, AF enables the subconcsious to recognise what is *genuinely* urgent and important to us now - which invariably (at least in my experience to date) can be very different.

    Try not to think too much about the items - or even why specific things are jumping out. If each time you see "Save world hunger" or whatever project/s you have listed, the fact that it in some bothers you is maybe that is an indication to you that it is important and your subconscious is recognising that. The fact that it feels like a big and seemingly impossible task does not mean it cannot be actioned. You only have to do a small amount to cross off the task - and can then readd it or the next step to the end of the list. That is how the lists keep moving - you are constantly cycling and recylcing and Mark has said somewhere that "the action takes place mostly towards the end of the lists". That fluidity keeps things moving (progressing) rather than allowing them to become stagnant.

    For example:

    World Hunger could become
    How can I help to alleviate world hunger?
    What charities are involved in this field?
    Which charities do I want to support?
    Do I want to be more directly involved?
    What organisations support/enable direct involvement? etc etc

    Each task may just require a few minutes to jot down a few ideas or to research organisations but they are moving you towards the real question for you.

    Good Luck Karin!

  • Hi Mark,

    You say:

    "Kevin is absolutely right in what he says. We make the decisions first and then rationalise them afterwards. There is plenty of scientific (and popularised) literature to show that this is the case."

    Are you saying that we always make decisions first and then rationalize them? Are you saying that the scientific literature shows that this is always true? I don't think either of those propositions is true. If they were, then that very idea itself would be the result of rationalized intuition and you'd have to expect that my accepting it would also be a matter of my rationalized intuition ... so there would scarcely be any point in writing it here ... or in fact, even even discussing the AF system as we would all be deciding and understanding based on our intuition anyway.

    As I see it, some of us have very little intuition at all (or, perhaps better put, are mostly deaf to the voice of our intuition). Others are more finely tuned to it. Still others act blindly on gut intuition, no matter the consequences, and seldom think about much at all. I have seen little evidence that we are all the same in this, or most any other facet of human functioning.

    You say:

    "But what needs to be added is that "intuition" is a product of our life experience and training. Studies of how people like fire chiefs or pilots make decisions in desperate circumstances shows that they don't go through any rational consideration process. Instead they act from their "gut-instinct". But the quality of the decisions they make through that "gut-instinct" depends on how much they have dealt with similar circumstances before."

    Yes, exactly. Intuition is that it is a product of learning and experience. If we try to act on intuition backed up by no experience we are in trouble. But the experience has to come from action and without intuition, in the early stages, we must think our way through the process to build that intuition we can use later. What is it that someone said: "Good judgment comes from bad experience. Bad experience comes from bad judgment." The trick is to think about what we are doing then evaluate our experience, thereby building the material upon which we can base intuition.

    The second part of it is the USE of intuition to guide action. That is also something learned. I'm not sure it can really be taught, one just has to try and see how something works out, take feedback, and try again. But in both the use of intuition and the content of intuition, there is a learning process involved. Intuition is not simply an innate and infallible process that can be switched on by an act of will and the be expected to produce good results. For those with little experience in using intuition, or little experience in the particular endeavor about to be attempted, the results of relying on intuition are likely to be disaster.

    One last thought. As I mentioned earlier, dichotomies are almost always wrong. There is no split between the mind and the body ... we are a single organism. There is no split between rational and intuitive ... they interact and feed into one another. What's the old joke? "There are two kinds of people: those who believe there are two kinds of people and those who don't." I have found, personally, that anytime I build a dichotomous proposition I end up scrapping it. And over the years, my scrap pile has gotten quite large.

  • Hi Mike, in response to some of your comments:
    > "Are you saying that we always make decisions first and then
    rationalize them? Are you saying that the scientific literature shows
    that this is always true?"

    A slightly more fitting theory in my opinion is that our rational 'conscious' facilities are called to evaluate possible solutions or decisions which are suggested by our 'unconscious' mind - what we call our intuition. However, I think the somewhat popular view of intuition as some special, rare facility (which only rears its head now and then and is inaccurate except in highly-trained people) is inaccurate. Intuition, to me, is all of our working mind, except the bits we feel conscious about. When you're playing chess, a series of possible moves are handed up to your brain, which you then evaluate slowly, consciously and logically. But where did the suggestions come from?
    When you walk up the stairs, what makes you place one foot in a certain place and shift your hips in another direction? This certainly isn't in your conscious mind.
    When driving down a quiet suburban street, what makes you pause suddenly, just before a poorly-visible child darts out from behind a parked car?

    > "As I see it, some of us have very little intuition at all (or, perhaps better put, are mostly deaf to the voice of our intuition). Others are more finely tuned to it. Still others act blindly on gut intuition, no matter the consequences, and seldom think about much at all. I have seen little evidence that we are all the same in this, or most any other facet of human functioning."

    I don't think anybody lacks intuition - it is such a critical part of human behaviour and reasoning that we would be useless without it. If the people you're thinking of can walk, dance, play music or tennis or whatever, then they have enormous capability for intuitive thought, just like the rest of us. Only a very small part of our behaviour comes from what we call the conscious mind. In fact, there may be no such thing as a conscious mind - Minsky suggests that it might just be an illusion whereby our short-term memory systems simulate one linear stream of thought (when in reality there were perhaps thousands or hundreds of thousands of competing, co-operating agents contributing). However, there are people who do choose to ignore many intuitive suggestions and reason many decisions from first principles to some extent. This is just misguided, IMO, and a gigantic waste of brainpower :)
    Note that it's very easy to overthink what should be simple decisions. See "Simple Heuristics That Make Us Smart" by Gigerenzer et al for some examples of decisions that are better made with LESS information (i.e. fast and frugal heuristics).

    > "Yes, exactly. Intuition is that it is a product of learning and experience. If we try to act on intuition backed up by no experience we are in trouble. But the experience has to come from action and without intuition, in the early stages, we must think our way through the process to build that intuition we can use later."

    Right, but if we do try to act on 'intuition' with no experience, we will almost certainly know that we're not doing the right thing. Intuitive processes aren't simply some black magic device where answers come from nowhere and we're left with no idea whether they are good or bad answers. They come directly from our knowledge, albeit not through some consciously-controlled process.
    So yes, when you have no prior experience in some area, you must rely on other agencies not designed for the job, as well as conscious logical reasoning and observation to guide not only the task at hand, but your learning process as you build agencies to support the new task (which happens quite quickly - remember when you started learning to touch-type and feeling that euphoric sensation as you noticed less and less conscious effort was being required to type, and you were doing it faster? This probably happened within 20 or 30 minutes).

    > "Intuition is not simply an innate and infallible process that can be switched on by an act of will and the be expected to produce good results. For those with little experience in using intuition, or little experience in the particular endeavor about to be attempted, the results of relying on intuition are likely to be disaster."

    Indeed - since intuitive thought obviously comes from our knowledge and skills, if we are seriously lacking in fundamental knowledge or understanding of a subject, we can't reasonably expect to make useful intuitive decisions about it (otherwise, intuition would be a mystical, infallible oracle demon which it is not).
    However, by making such decisions anyway and observing the results, (even if we initially reject them repeatedly until we find decisions that we can rationally validate) we will build up such knowledge, even if we don't know how to express that knowledge clearly. That's what our brains do.
    This is why I keep practicing the piano even though I was extremely poor at the beginning. If you never practice (which, in the case of music performance and most sports (where the agencies involved are far removed from our high-level 'rational' reasoning processes), involves learning things which are difficult to express in natural 'rational' language) you will not improve. If you do practice, you will improve, even if
    you're not really sure why.

    A great book which treats this topic is "The Society of Mind" by Marvin Minsky - well worth reading! Good reading in these comments too :)
